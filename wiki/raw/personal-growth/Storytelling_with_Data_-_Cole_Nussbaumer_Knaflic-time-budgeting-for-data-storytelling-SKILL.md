# Time Budgeting for Data Storytelling

## When to Use This Skill
Use this skill **after completing data analysis** and **before finalizing reports or presentations**, specifically when:
- Your audience includes stakeholders outside your immediate team
- The goal is to influence decisions, not just share exploratory insights
- You have a defined objective and audience context

Do **not** use this skill for purely internal, exploratory analyses with no external communication requirement.

## How to Execute

1. **Acknowledge the visibility gap**: Recognize that only the communication phase is seen by your audience—the entire analytical process (data collection, cleaning, modeling) remains invisible.

2. **Reject default visualizations**: Understand that out-of-the-box chart settings lack narrative intent and rarely serve explanatory purposes effectively.

3. **Budget explicit time blocks** for each storytelling subtask:
   - Understand audience context and motivation
   - Craft a clear 3-minute story and Big Idea
   - Explore multiple visual representations of key insights
   - Declutter visuals using Gestalt principles (proximity, similarity, continuity)
   - Apply pre-attentive attributes (color, size, position) to guide attention
   - Iterate based on feedback

4. **Adjust expectations**: Allocate as much time for storytelling as was spent on prior analytical steps combined—it often takes longer than anticipated.

5. **Avoid the "graph-and-go" trap**: Do not consider communication complete once data is plotted.

6. **Prioritize under constraints**: If timelines are tight, focus on high-impact incremental improvements such as:
   - Adding a clear, insight-driven headline
   - Removing non-data ink (e.g., borders, gridlines)
   - Highlighting the key takeaway visually

## Expected Output
A revised project timeline that includes protected, non-negotiable time blocks dedicated to data storytelling refinement.
