Abstract

Since the reform and opening-up policy, especially since the 18th
National Congress of the Communist Party of China, China\'s rural
financial reform has continuously deepened. It has actively played a
role in supporting agriculture, rural areas, and farmers, achieving
positive results in supporting the precise implementation of poverty
alleviation strategies, realizing the comprehensive development of rural
areas, and advancing comprehensive rural revitalization. The 20th
National Congress of the Communist Party of China proposed the goal of
\"comprehensively promoting rural revitalization\" and the need to
\"improve the rural financial service system,\" providing further
guidance for the reform and development of rural finance in the new era.
Currently, in light of the diverse and multifaceted financial service
demands required for rural modernization, China\'s rural financial
sector still needs to address some deep-rooted issues, such as
inadequate and outdated infrastructure, incomplete and
difficult-to-collect agricultural data, insufficient supply of rural
financial products and services, and a pressing need to enhance service
efficiency and quality.

Digital rural development is a strategic direction for rural
revitalization, and the exploration of the integration of digital
technology and rural finance holds significant importance in practical
financial services for rural revitalization. Long-term observation shows
that the application of digital technology in rural finance can
effectively address long-standing issues in rural financial development,
such as high transaction costs, information asymmetry, and difficulties
in financing. This has great significance in promoting rural
revitalization, fostering shared prosperity, and reducing the
urban-rural wealth gap.

In this dissertation, centering on the application of digital technology
in rural financial development, we firstly analyze the challenges and
reasons for the difficulties in rural financial development in China.
Through empirical research, the paper concludes that there is a positive
correlation between the level of rural financial development and the
coverage of rural internet users in various provinces across the
country. This suggests that the widespread use of digital technology in
rural areas and among rural households is associated with a higher level
of the positive development of rural finance. The paper also discusses
the advantages of rural financial digitalization from a theoretical
perspective and explains the mechanisms through which it promotes
development at the infrastructure, market, and policy levels.

Secondly, we expound on the applications of Internet in rural finance.
There are four major applications of Internet: comprehensive e-commerce
channel sinking, building an integrated agricultural financial ecosystem
composed of leading agricultural enterprises, interlinking of large P2P
platforms with the agricultural supply chain, and traditional financial
institutions developing the "Digital Plus Rural Finance" business mode.

Finally, While the application of digital technology can effectively
drive the new development of rural finance, it still faces challenges
and constraints. These include the lack of a robust rural credit system
and issues related to the accessibility and utilization of new digital
financial products and services in remote rural areas. To address these
challenges, it is necessary for the government to accelerate research
and implement a coordinated plan for further rural digital
infrastructure, strengthen the innovation of digital inclusive financial
products, and enhance the digital financial literacy of rural residents.
This will allow the full potential of digital technology tools in rural
finance to be realized.This dissertation, based on the use of rural
financial digitalization to support the future development of rural
revitalization, provides practical and feasible development
recommendations with significant guiding value.

Key words: Digitalization; rural finance; rural credit; Rural
Revitalization

Chapter 1 Introduction

### 1.1 Research Motivation

The implementation of the rural revitalization strategy and the
innovation of rural financial services in the new era are highly
compatible. Rural revitalization cannot do without the support of rural
financial innovation, and, conversely, it can promote the development of
rural finance. The mutual promotion and organic unity of these elements
will strongly drive sustainable rural economic development. On one hand,
rural financial innovation offers an important guarantee for achieving
rural revitalization by providing new financial products, payment
methods, and financing channels. On the other hand, rural revitalization
expands the overall size of the rural real economy, thereby providing a
solid economic foundation for rural financial innovation.

In China, although rural financial institutions are not yet fully
developed, the government has made significant efforts to address rural
financial issues. However, instances of \"market failure\" and
\"government failure\" persist, especially in traditional rural
financial models. Issues such as capital outflows and \"expensive
financing\" hinder the comprehensive implementation of the rural
revitalization strategy. To address these shortages, the concept of
\"inclusive finance\" was introduced, yet a significant gap remains
between rural financial development and inclusivity. The 20th National
Congress of the Communist Party of China further emphasized the need to
improve the rural financial service system. Under this backdrop, the
smooth progress of rural financial digitalization is key to providing
financial support for rural revitalization. This study delves into the
relationship between rural finance and digital applications from a
theoretical perspective, aiming to create social value by integrating
theory with practice. The study aims to understand how digital finance
can effectively support rural finance, providing developmental insights
and theoretical support for various fields.

### 1.2 Methodology and Results

This dissertation, based on an extensive reading of relevant literature
and books, explores the relationship and methods of using digital
technology to develop rural finance. The research approach integrates
theory and practice to ensure that the suggested improvements are
effective.

The study adopts a literature review method, where the paper extensively
collects and organizes a wide array of books, literature, and data
related to the application of digital technology in rural financial
development. This synthesis establishes a strong theoretical foundation
and supports the formulation of reasonable solutions. Furthermore, the
paper employs quantitative analysis to examine the impact of digital
technology adoption on rural financial development. To validate the
hypothesis that higher levels of digital technology adoption are
positively associated with better rural financial development, the study
uses empirical data from Chinese rural areas from 2009 to 2016 for
quantitative empirical analysis. Additionally, the paper utilizes a case
study method to investigate the development of rural financial
digitalization in the context of rural revitalization. Through case
analysis, the paper derives relevant insights and formulates
recommendations for using digital technology to promote rural inclusive
finance.

The research concludes that there is a positive correlation between the
level of rural financial development and the coverage of rural internet
users in various provinces across the country. The empirical results
effectively validate the basic assumption of this study, suggesting that
the widespread use of digital technology in rural areas and among rural
households is a significant factor associated with the positive
development of rural finance.

Through empirical research, the paper demonstrates the positive
relationship between digitalization and rural finance, and provides an
explanation of the operational mechanism through which digitalization
promotes development at its roots. The research confirms that digital
applications significantly improve and optimize rural financial
innovation, helping to activate the endogenous capabilities of farmers
for income growth and enhance agricultural production efficiency. The
analysis also reveals the causes of the current state of rural financial
development from the angles of infrastructure, market, and government.

### 1.3 Possible Innovations and Limitations

[]{#_Toc520550272 .anchor}The significance of this research primarily

theoretical framework that focuses on the advantages of digital
applications in rural finance. This foundation provides a fresh approach

# Chapter 2 Literature Review

### 2.1 Western Studies 

#### 2.1.1 Studies on Digital Finance

In Western countries, digital finance does not exist as a separate
sector. Most Western scholars and industry experts assert that Digital
finance is a part of the financial sector and thus largely embodies the
functions of financial intermediary. Digital finance emerged from
practice and is a newborn thing, so little theoretical research has been
done on Digital finance. Digital finance appeared earlier in Western
countries, where many scholars and experts have ever introduced their
own views on digital finance. Leland and Pyle (1977) proposed the
concept of financial intermediary, claiming that financial intermediary
is an alliance that can facilitate information sharing. Later, many
experts and scholars worked on improving the concepts of financial
intermediary and digital finance. For instance, Diamond (1983) argued
that financial intermediary alliance is composed of individual
investors. Merton and Bodie (1993) recognized the potential of digital
finance, demonstrating that financial intermediaries represented by
digital finance could accurately collect and process information, which
is the precondition for a good financial institution to function
properly. Diamond and Rajan (2001) reported that financial
intermediaries pay more attention to their reputation and possible
financing margins when fulfilling their duties. In the following year,
Berger and Udell conducted a monographic study on the contractual
relationship between financial intermediaries and enterprises, reporting
that counterparties under whatsoever relationships are, without
exception, convinced of the importance of information. With continuous
research and attempt as well as the upgrade of financial products,
modern financial theory is gradually taking shape.

In recent years, as digital finance evolved by leaps and bounds, digital
finance in developed countries such as Europe and the United States has
gradually become standardized, institutionalized and self-regulated.
Especially after President Obama signed the *Jump Start Our Business
Startups Act* (hereinafter referred to as the JOBS Act) in 2012, digital
finance is getting mature in Europe and the United State. There has been
growing interest in digital finance and some valuable research findings
have been reported by more experts and scholars. These studies mainly
deal with the development and mode of digital finance, the
characteristics and transaction behaviors of borrowers, lender
behaviors, and risk and regulation.

Studies on the development of digital finance. Western studies on the
development of digital finance include: Meyer and Heng (2007) introduced
the concept of P2P. Reil (2007) contended that the application of Web
2.0 technology provides a technical support for the development of
digital financial platforms. Slavin (2007) stated that the application
of digital technology has significantly curtailed and even eliminated
the operating cost of online lending platforms, the transaction cost of
customers, the time cost of counterparties and the intermediation cost
of mortgage guarantee.

Johnson (2010) found that the low-cost network technology has fostered a
potential large customer base for the popularization of P2P lending.
Berger (2007) theorized that digital financing platforms can help to
reduce information asymmetry and achieve win-win for both borrowers and
lenders. Wang (2009) stated that the financing mode of integrating
e-commerce with finance will reshuffle the existing loan market. Studies
on the transaction behaviors of digital finance. There are abundant
studies on the transaction behaviors of digital finance abroad, most of
which focus on the behaviors of the participants in P2P lending.

Studies on borrower behaviors. Ryan (2008) asserted that borrowers'
financial and non-financial information may influence the loan approval
rate. According to Freedman and Jin (2008), a borrower may find it
easier to get a loan and close the transaction if he furnishes the
investor more precise and detailed financial information. In the view of
Herzenstein (2008), the borrower's financial strength is an important
factor underlying the loan approval rate. Klafft (2008) found that the
loan/income ratio and debt ratio exert a significant impact on the loan
interest, whereas the proof of collateral ownership has little impact on
loan pricing. Klafft (2008) also found that credit score has a
significant impact on the loan approval rate. Iyer et al. (2009)
supported that conclusion. Krumme and Herrero-Lopes (2009) argued that
the higher the credit score, the easier it will be for the borrower to
get a loan. Puro (2010) contended that the loan approval rate is
negatively correlated with the loan amount and interest rate. Collier
(2010) supported that argument. Kumar (2007) found that investors
recover risk compensation from borrowers depending the non-performing
loan ratio. Freedman & Jin (2008) also asserted that default notes would
lower the borrower's credit rating and in turn influence the loan
application, loan rate and loan amount. Ravina (2008) maintained that
demographic characteristics (race, sex, age, etc.) may influence the
transaction behaviors in the P2P lending market. Pope (2008) further
confirmed this conclusion. Pope & Sydnor (2008) classified
discrimination into taste-based discrimination and statistical
discrimination. Pope & Sydnor (2008), Ravina (2012), Pope & Sydnor
(2008), Pope & Sydnor (2008), Chen (2014) and Barasinska (2009) studied
borrower behaviors from the aspects of race, age and gender
respectively, concluding that these demographic characteristics may
significantly influence the loan approval rate and loan rate. On the
contrary, Andres (2008), Pope & Sydnor (2008), Ravina (2012) and other
scholars argued that demographic characteristics have a negligible
impact on the loan approval rate compared with the borrower's financial
factors. In addition, some scholars have studied non-financial
information except demographic characteristics. Lin (2009) examined the
relationship between loan purpose and loan approval rate and loan rate.
Ana Ceci-lia (2008) and Michels (2012) suggested that P2P lending is
accompanied with information filtering cost. Herzenstein et al. (2011)
found that borrowers' self-recommendation is negatively correlated with
investors' willingness to lend. Kumar (2010) argued that group
reputation has no significant impact on "good" borrowers.

Researchers on lender behaviors. Kumar (2010) claimed that investors'
behaviors conform to the "rational-economic man" hypothesis. Sonenshein
(2010) found that social capital has a significant impact on investors'
decision-making. Samuel (2008) reported that "search & bidding" is a key
factor in investors' investment decision-making. Puro (2010) contended
that herd mentality is commonly found with investors in the bidding
process. Asch (1956) introduced the herd behavior effect of investors.
Scholars like Krumme & Herrero (2009), Herzenstein et al. (2010), Ceyhan
et al. (2011) and Lee et al. (2012) supported that herd behavior is
common in P2P lending transactions. As to the reasons behind herd
behavior, Krumme & Herrero (2009) found that once the first investor
bids, the bidding process will speed up rapidly. Herzenstein et al.
(2010) asserted that the amount of proposals is an important factor
inducing herd behavior. In the view of Shen et al. (2010), social
capital is also one of the factors causing herd behavior. Ceyhan et al.
(2011) and Lee et al. (2012) found that the impact of participation rate
on subsequent investment is diminishing. Zhang et al. (2012) argued that
rational herd behavior exists in the United States' P2P lending market.
And yet, Wang & Greiner (2010) and Luo & Lin (2013) asserted that
irrational herd behavior exists in the market. Studies on social
capital. Putnam (1993) introduced the concept of social capital. Some
scholars investigated the impact of social capital of the lender in a
P2P lending platform, namely "soft information", on the loan
availability and cost. For example, Ming Fing Lin (2009) demonstrated
that relational social capital can alleviate information asymmetry in
the lending process. Greiner et al. further confirmed this conclusion.
Freedman (2010) probed into the causes of information asymmetry in the
P2P lending market. Sergio Herrero-Lopez (2009) examined the impact of
social interaction on credit behaviors, concluding that social
interaction can raise the loan approval rate. Studies on the model of
digital finance.

Western studies on the operating model of P2P lending platforms mainly
concentrate on those successful P2P lending platforms. Greiner & Wang
(2009) and Lin et al. reported that the Prosper credit rating system is
relatively mature with well-established pre-loan and post-loan rules.
Hulme (2006) concluded through a case study of Zope that Zope provides
transparent and real financial services. Emma (2011) made a comparative
analysis on the different types of P2P platforms in Europe and concluded
that Zope's success lies in risk control. Studies on the risks and
regulation of digital finance. Freedman & Jin (2008) found that the
borrower would deliberately conceal unfavorable information to induce
the lender to grant loans, thereby intentionally producing information
asymmetry and resulting in adverse selection. But with a wealth of
trading experience, investors can easily discover such information
asymmetry. Johnson (2010) argued that a group can also solve the problem
of information asymmetry by establishing an appropriate incentive
mechanism. Weiss et al. (2010) suggested that the "anonymity" of the
digital exacerbates information asymmetry. Therefore, P2P lending
platforms' verification of borrower information can substantially ease
the problem of adverse selection. As to the studies on the regulation of
P2P lending platforms, some scholars hold that the problem of P2P
platform regulation must be solved.

Xiao Guang & Yi (2011) claimed that P2P platforms must be subject to
financial regulation.

A brief comment on Western studies on digital finance: Western studies
mainly deal with the development and mode of digital finance, the
characteristics and transaction behaviors of borrowers, lender
behaviors, and risk and regulation. The latest theoretical findings with
respect to the development and mode of digital finance include: the
concepts of P2P lending and crowdfunding were defined, and the
application of digital technology and the mediating effect of Internet
platform were investigated through case study of Prosper and Zope,
striking a cornerstone for the rapid development of digital finance. The
latest theoretical findings regarding the transaction behaviors in the
digitalfinance market include: empirical studies on the transaction
behaviors in the digital finance market from the borrower's perspective
indicate that the more detailed the information furnished by the
borrower, the stronger the financial strength, the higher the credit
score, the clearer the use of loans and the better the group's
reputation, the higher the loan approval rate and loan rate will be.
However, the loan amount, capital price, default rate and borrower
self-recommendation are negatively correlated with the loan approval
rate and loan rate. In addition, demographic characteristics and gender
are also related to the loan approval rate and loan rate. Empirical
studies from the investor's point of view suggest the investor's
behaviors conform to the "rational-economic man" hypothesis, herd
behavior exists in the P2P lending market, and the impact of
participation rate on subsequent investment follows the law of
diminishing in economics. Equally important, the studies also imply that
social capital, i.e., soft information, has a significant impact on the
loan approval rate. The higher the softness of information, the more
unreal and unreliable the signals sent, the lower the loan approval rate
and the higher the loan rate will be. In other words, information
softness is negatively correlated with loan approval rate and capital
price. The latest theoretical findings regarding risks and regulation of
digital finance include: Western studies on digital finance show that
the "anonymity" of participants in the digital financial market
exacerbate the risk of information asymmetry, but the problem of
information asymmetry can be solved by establishing an incentive
mechanism. In addition, researchers conclude through studies on the
risks of digital finance that P2P lending platforms should be subject to
financial regulation.

Generally speaking, Western studies on digital finance have yielded a
considerable amount of research findings, providing a theoretical
guidance for the sustainable and sound development of digital finance.
However, there are also many limitations in these Western studies.
Firstly, the studies on the development and mode of digital finance are
primarily concerned with the development of P2P lending and
crowdfunding, while few of them are attempted to explore other online
financing models and study their development. Secondly, most scholars
select Prosper and Zope as sample platforms, leading to poor
representativeness and replicability of samples. Thirdly, Western
studies on the participants in the digital financial market focus on
particular participants but rarely probe into the participants'
behaviors as a whole. Fourthly, there is still dearth of research on the
prevention of risks associated with digital finance and regulation of
online lending platforms.

#### 2.1.2 Studies on Rural Finance

There are few literatures on rural finance and rural economy in Western
countries. And the representative ones mainly concentrate on the
efficiency of financial instruments and financial risk aversion in
developing countries. For example, Bain (1992) stated that micro
financial efficiency mainly refers to the utilization of financial
instruments, while macro financial efficiency refers to the contribution
of the entire banking industry to macroeconomic stability. Galor, Zeira,
Banerjee & Newman (1993) found that financial development and economic
growth cannot bridge the income gap in underdeveloped financial markets,
and this goal can be attained only by improving the rural financial
market. In terms of financial instrument services, Frank Hllinger (2011)
argued that although credit quotas and collaterals can be used to reduce
the risks of rural financial instruments and operating costs, commercial
financial institutions are apt to consider the problem of insufficient
collateral and are less likely to provide more services to the rural
financial market. Likewise, Meyer (2011) claimed that it is very
difficult to provide financial services to rural areas in developing
countries since financial institutions are less inclined to do so in
consideration of interest cost. In fact, the biggest problem of
providing services to the rural financial market lies in financial
regulation and risk control. Steel and Andah (2008) surveyed the
supervision over the microfinance market and concluded that the cost of
supervision is too high. Gutirrez-Niet et al. (2007) analyzed the
attributes of microfinance institutions, reporting that they not only
have social attributes but also yield profits. They further concluded
through quantitative analysis that their identity as non-governmental
organization is the key factor affecting the utilization of microcredit
funds. Again, some scholars stressed the necessity to regulate
microfinance services with laws and regulations. Arun (2005) claimed
that appropriate rules and regulations help to increase the efficiency
of microfinance institutions and fulfill their diversified functions.

On the other hand, some scholars theorize that rural finance may
influence agricultural output, and the efficiency of rural finance
should be measured by the agricultural output it contributes. In 1990,
Michael T. Belongia and R. Alton Cilbert created an analytical model for
assessing the impact of rural finance on agricultural output in a bid to
analyze the relationship between the two. In the same year, J. D. von
Piske et al. developed a variety of functions and linear programming to
test the relationship between rural finance and agricultural output. The
in-depth analysis of scholars and experts has generated wide interest in
rural finance from all sectors of society. In 2002, two heavyweights
from the USDA Agricultural Research Service and the FED pointed out that
rural finance needs to adapt to the financial market environment of the
new age in the shortest possible time in order to facilitate economic
growth and maintain social stability by serving the rural areas. In the
same year, Indian scholars studied the impacts of rural banking policy
changes in India over the past 40 years and found that the rise and
development of rural banks contributed to significant improvements of
the local economy. Despite of the wide recognition of rural finance,
people also realize that quite a few problems with rural finance have
yet to be addressed. In Jun. 2001, the Caribbean Development Bank
explicitly pointed out that there are problems in operation of rural
financial organizations which cannot be radically solved by government
intervention. Many Western scholars believe that the studies on the
rural financial system in developing countries are of high value
considering the particularity of developing countries' economic
development. And these studies have also made considerable achievements.
Eze et al. (2010) analyzed the Nigerian government's agricultural
financial policy, demonstrating that although the government has made a
lot of policy efforts, the budget allocation to rural areas is still
limited, which might lead to corruption. Junior R. Davis (1998) studied
Romania's rural financial problems quantitatively, reporting that rural
residents' income is the main factor affecting their loans and savings,
in addition to some other factors such as the use of loans. John James
(2013) investigated rural finance in China and pointed out that the
corresponding fiscal and taxation policies should be analyzed at
village, township and county levels. Jensen (2005) found through
empirical studies that the government-led agricultural credit system in
developing countries is relatively inefficient in promoting agricultural
investment. On the contrary, the market-oriented financing mode combined
with moderate and necessary intervention practiced in developed
countries are more efficient.

### 2.2 Chinese Studies

#### 2.2.1 Studies on digital Finance

Chinese studies on digital finance deal mainly with the development,
models, transaction behaviors, risks and supervision of digital finance
as well as the comparison with traditional banking and Western models.

Studies on the development of digital finance. Scholars have different
opinions on the definition of digital finance. Xie Ping et al. (2012)
introduced the concept of digital finance in *A Study on Digital Finance
Model*, expounding on its connotation and extension and suggesting that
digital finance is a "third" financing model different from commercial
banking and capital market. Ma Yun (2013) argued that the engagement of
digital enterprises in financing gives rise to digital finance, while
the operation of traditional financial institutions creates financial
networking. Wu Xiaoling (2013) contended that the essence of digital
finance is to use the digital and information technology to process and
transmit financial information, conduct financial operations, build
channels and complete the accommodation of funds. Qian Jinye et al.
(2012) claimed that P2P lending originated from the folk Rotating

A brief comment on Chinese studies on digital finance: Chinese studies
deal mainly with the development, models, transaction behaviors, risks
and regulation of digital finance as well as the comparison with
traditional banks and Western models. By studying the development and
models of digital finance in China, the scholars defined the concept of
digital finance, probed into the drivers of the development of digital
finance in China, the impact of digital finance on traditional banking
and its development trend, and put forward five main operating models of
digital finance in China (third-party payment, online microfinance, P2P
lending, crowdfunding and Bitcoin). By studying transaction behaviors,
the researchers analyzed the characteristics of transaction behaviors of
lenders and borrowers in the lending business, such as borrowers'
credit, educational background and historical loan records. The studies
on risks and regulation revealed that digital finance not only embodies
the risk profiles of digital and finance but also exhibits its own
special risk features, such as risk superposition, personal information
leakage, network security, etc. Studies proposed that we should not only
encourage innovation in the digital financial market but also intensify
financial supervision and self-regulation of the industry by
establishing standard and well-designed laws and regulations governing
digital finance. Through comparison with traditional banking, the
researchers pointed out the advantages of digital finance in SME
financing and identified its shortcomings. By comparing with the
development of digital finance abroad, Chinese scholars suggested that
we should learn from the merits of digital finance development in
Western countries and meanwhile develop digital finance depending on
China's actualities so as to ensure digital finance has Chinese
characteristics and can truly ease SMEs' financing difficulties. The
studies on digital finance done by Chinese experts and scholars have
yielded some theoretical results, which provides a theoretical reference
for the formulation of laws, regulations and regulatory rules of digital
finance in China and in turn drives the rapid development of digital
finance in China. However, there are also many limitations in the
Chinese studies on digital finance. Firstly, most of the Chinese studies
focus on the operation of digital finance, while little work has been
done to intensively study the theory of digital finance. Secondly, the
studies on transaction behaviors of digital finance are primarily
concerned with the unilateral behaviors of a particular participant,
providing little information on the behaviors of all participants.
Thirdly, the studies on risks and regulation of digital finance address
only the question of risk features, whereas few of them propose the
concrete measures of risk mitigation and regulation. Fourthly, the
studies on comparison with traditional banking and Western models
principally concentrate on the comparative advantages of digital
finance, whereas the risks and shortcomings of digital finance are less
studied. As to the comparison with Western models, most scholars explore
the advantages of digital finance abroad but put few efforts in
examining China's national conditions, social system, history and
culture.

#### 2.2.2 Studies on Rural Finance

Chinese scholars and experts have conducted very comprehensive studies
on the development of digital finance, producing are a wealth of
literatures on this topic. Most of the literatures focus on the impact
of rural finance on rural economic development, especially the impact of
on the increase of farmers' income. For example, Wu Daihong and Wang
Dingxiang (2013) found that the development of rural finance can
expedite the process of agricultural modernization and improve labor
productivity. Liu Yuchun and Xiu Changbai (2013) claimed that the
development of rural finance and the advancement of agricultural science
and technology can significantly increase farmers' income. Huo Yan
(2013) studied the relationship between farmers' income and rural
finance development, especially the impact of agricultural insurance and
agricultural investment funds on growth of farmers' income. Li Mingxian
et al. (2014) made an empirical analysis on the impact of various
factors underlying rural finance development on farmers' income. They
found that the rise of loan-to-deposit ratio of rural financial
institutions has a significant effect on farmers' income growth, but the
increase of deposits and the rise of loan ratio of township enterprises
cannot increase farmers' income. Therefore, they suggested that we
should refrain from blindly increasing the number of financial
institutions in the reform of rural finance. Zhang Bing and Weng Chen
(2015) not only analyzed the impact of rural finance and rural economic
development on poverty alleviation but also probed into the impact of
rural residents' educational background on poverty alleviation. Ding
Zhiguo et al. (2016) investigated the development path of rural finance
in China and asserted that the development of rural economy relies on
sound rural financial services, and more importantly, government policy
support and guidance. Zhao Hongdan (2016) studied the necessity of rural
finance for shoring up rural economic development in China, established
a long-term equilibrium relationship between rural finance and rural
economy, and pointed out that government and market are the main factors
affecting rural finance development and that financial supply should be
bolstered in underdeveloped areas. Zhang Rong (2017) analyzed the impact
of rural finance development on farmers' income, developed an evaluation
system of rural financial services, and theorized through empirical
analysis that the improvement of rural financial services is conducive
to increasing farmers' income. Some other scholars studied the impact of
rural finance development on reducing the urban-rural income disparity
from the perspective of urban-rural integration. For example, Xiang Ping
(2009) made a case study of Changsha, Zhuzhou and Xiangtan to analyze
the urban-rural dual structure as a setback to urban and rural economic
development, developed a model of urban and rural coordinated
development, and introduced an evaluation index system of urban and
rural coordinated development. Zhang Hongyan et al. (2013) made an
empirical study on the impact of rural finance development on the
urban-rural income gap in China, concluding that the widening
urban-rural income gap is mainly fueled by rural capital outflow and
inadequate efforts in promoting the development of informal financial
organizations. Feng Sixian (2014) studied the impact of rural financial
exclusion on the income gap between urban and rural residents,
suggesting that reasonable financial competition should be encouraged in
developed areas, while policy support should be given to underdeveloped
areas in a bid to narrow the income gap between urban and rural
residents. Yang Deping and Sheng Wen (2014) suggested that we should
work toward urban-rural integration, give full play to the functions of
rural finance, promote the development of formal and informal financial
institutions for complementation of the two. Xie Shiming (2015) found
that the development of rural finance will increase the income gap
between urban and rural residents in underdeveloped areas. Only when the
size of the economy rises to a certain level can it act as a motivator.
Yang Deyong, Yue Chuan and Bai Ningrui (2016) surveyed the differences
of rural finance in Beijing, Tianjin and Hebei based on the fractal
theory. They found that the development of rural finance follows a
non-linear rule and different regions vary much in the satisfaction of
needs for financial services, so we should adopt different strategies
depending on different regions and financial environments.

Some scholars have attempted to select specific agricultural development
areas as samples to explore the development process or characteristics
of local rural finance and then put forward corresponding
countermeasures and future development models. Li Xiangyang (2012)
examined the status and characteristics of rural finance development in
Sichuan Province and built a "circle-level" guiding planning system for
rural finance development. Ma Yan'an (2015) studied the rural finance
development in Northeast China, analyzed the limitations of rural
finance in this area, and established a framework and proposed
suggestions for future financial development in this area. Zhang Xin
(2016) investigated the relationship between rural finance development
and rural economy in Chinese poverty-stricken areas, suggesting that we
should strengthen innovation in rural finance and improve the rural
policy support system and financial operation environment.

In addition, some studies focus on the reform and development model of
rural financial institutions. For instance, Wang Xin (2014) studied the
development characteristics of emerging rural financial institutions in
China, specially compared and analyzed the characteristics of mutual
funds and microfinance companies, and proposed the alternative models of
sustainable rural finance in future. Zhang Ning et al. (2016) examined
the impact of rural finance incremental reform on credit supply using
panel data. They reported that such impact varies among different
regions with the size of economy and is greater in economically
developed regions. These existing Chinese and Western studies have laid
a solid theoretical and empirical foundation for the theoretical
construction of rural finance and economic development. Most studies are
intended to study the role of rural finance in boosting rural economy
and explore diversified models of rural finance. Especially when
discussing how to narrow the income gap between urban and rural
residents, many scholars suggest that we should not simply increase
financial supply but carry out rural financial reform depending on local
economic development and local conditions.

### 2.3 Summary of Available Literatures

As revealed by the available studies, because of the differences in
social system and financial sector, rural digital finance in China is a
research field completely different from that in Western countries.
Western studies are not likely to study digital finance as an industry
and rarely regard rural digital finance as a research direction. Along
with the rapid development of digital finance in China over the past few
years, digital finance has become a favorite topic for analysis and
rural digital finance is evolving accordingly. However, there are many
problems in the development of rural digital finance, mainly involving
credit data and farmers' fundamental cognition. The root of these
problems lies in the underdeveloped infrastructure of digital finance in
rural areas (including but not limited to data collection and financial
knowledge popularization). In this dissertation, based on the available
studies, we will improve the relevant definitions theoretically and
elaborate the relationship among rural finance, financial inclusion and
digital finance. In addition, we will discuss the role of the digital in
driving the development of rural finance through empirical analysis, and
explain the rationale for its functioning as a spur from three aspects.
Finally, we will put forward relevant development proposals based on the
current situation of Chinese rural finance and digital application.

# Chapter 3 Theoretical Basis

### 3.1 Definitions of Relevant Concepts

#### 3.1.1 Definition of Rural Finance

Finance is defined as the accommodation of monetary funds, generally
referring to all activities related to monetary circulation and bank
credit. In the view of Wang Shaoyi, rural finance refers to the
accommodation of rural monetary funds. At present, the development of
rural finance is laggard in China, as evident by serious financial
exclusion and "market & policy failure", which cannot meet the needs of
rural economic transformation and modern agricultural development. Thus,
we must actively seek countermeasures to accelerate the development of
rural finance.

The components of China's rural financial system mainly include service
demanders, service providers and regulators, which are described as
follows:

\(1\) Service demanders. The main demanders of rural finance are
"agriculture, rural areas and farmers", including loan services required
for agricultural production; loan services for farmers' farming
activities, weddings and funerals, medical treatment, education and
retirement as well as deposit, payment, bank transfer and financial
planning services; and loan services for road reconstruction.

\(2\) Service providers. Relevant financial institutions are the
providers of financial services in rural areas, including rural credit
cooperatives, agricultural development banks, postal savings banks,
agricultural banks, rural financial institutions and insurance
institutions, among which, rural credit cooperatives are the dominant
providers.

\(3\) Regulators. The CBRC is responsible for regulation of rural
financial services in China. In general, the supervision task is carried
out by county-level primary regulatory offices.

#### 3.1.2 Definition of Financial Inclusion

In seek of maximized profits, financial institutions tend to prefer the
rich over the poor, which has become a common phenomenon. Under the
harsh market environment, what they can do is just to "add flowers to
the brocade" instead of "offering fuel in snowy weather ". Therefore,
most of the poor are excluded from financial services, which not only
aggravates the polarization between the rich and the poor and impedes
economic development but also affects social stability. In economics
terms, the purpose of resource allocation is to improve efficiency, that
is, to make the best possible use of men and materials. However, such
market-based unfair and unbalanced allocation of financial resources is
actually a phenomenon of market failure. The "financial inclusion"
concept was introduced to solve this market failure and achieve
efficient allocation of resources. It is necessary to not only eliminate
financial exclusion but also eradicate the concept of financial
discrimination.

\(1\) Concept of financial inclusion

Financial inclusion refers to the pursuit of making financial services
accessible to all individuals and groups in an efficient and all-round
way. This concept was first introduced at the 2005 International Annual
Conference on Microfinance, in which Mr. Annan, then Secretary-General
of the United Nations, said: "We are facing a grim reality that most of
the world's poor populations still do not have access to sustainable
financial services, such as savings, credit and insurance; a great
challenge before us is to address the constraints of total participation
in the financial sector\... In short, we can and must establish an
inclusive financial system to improve people's lives." In 2006, Jiao
Jinpu, Director of the Financial Consumer Protection Department of the
People's Bank of China, was the first to put forward the concept of
"inclusive financial system" in China. In his opinion, the inclusive
financial system should cover all individuals in the society and provide
products needed by all. This system is not simply poverty alleviation
but is built on the endogenous power of government assistance and market
mechanism. And it is commercially sustainable. Financial inclusion can
resist financial discrimination. The rich have easy access to
comprehensive financial services, so the focus of financial inclusion is
to make financial services available to the unprivileged groups so that
they can have access to efficient and good financial services at an
affordable cost with dignity, thereby giving full play to their roles in
advancing economic development, avoiding economic shocks and realizing
common prosperity.

\(2\) Components of inclusive financial system

The inclusive financial system is composed mainly of service providers,
service demanders, service products, financial supervision, supply and
demand characteristics, and sustainability.

\(a\) Service providers. Service providers include private commercial
banks, social goal-oriented public or private institutions, state-owned
commercial banks and savings banks, postal banks, private and
state-owned rural banks, banks or non-bank financial intermediaries,
credit cooperatives, banks or other financial institutions, and other
non-governmental organizations under formal supervision.

\(b\) Service demanders. Service demanders are divided into three types:
first, customers having access to formal financial services; second,
customers having no access to formal financial services but can obtain
micro-financial services; third, customers having no access to financial
services. Financial services have been made accessible to the first type
of customers, so the focus remains on the second and third types of
customers, mainly including disadvantaged areas, week industries and
unprivileged groups. The disadvantaged areas include sparsely-populated,
remote and poor areas with inconvenient transportation conditions; the
week industries include small and micro businesses, agriculture,
forestry, animal husbandry and fishery; and the unprivileged groups
include farmers and urban low-income population. As can be seen from the
above discussion, rural areas, agriculture and farmers are the key
service targets of financial inclusion.

\(c\) Service products. Households and businesses can have access to
various financial services at an affordable cost, mainly including
financial savings, loans, payment and settlement, risk management,
exchange, etc. Financial savings services include savings accounts,
stock accounts, treasury bonds, crowdfunding and so on. Loan services
refer to the provision of loan services for solvent and credible
individuals or businesses; payment and settlement services include
electronic settlement such as Alipay, credit card and debit card
services; risk management services refer to the provision of endowment
insurance, accident insurance, medical insurance, natural disaster
insurance, investment insurance, etc.; and exchange services include
regional and international exchange services.

\(d\) Supply and demand characteristics. We should seek for safety,
simplicity, rapidity, low cost and low limit. Safety implies the
protection of consumers' capital and personal information security;
simplicity means minimum data is required for operation so as to ensure
operability by the less educated; rapidity implies fast transaction and
smooth borrowing and repayment channels, making financial services
accessible at any time; low cost refers to low loan interest rate and
service charge; low limit requires us to lower or remove all the
admittance thresholds to make financial services fairly accessible to
all.\
Fifth, financial supervision.

\(e\) Financial supervision. We should establish and improve financial
regulators to supervise financial institutions in accordance with
relevant industry standards of financial supervision and meanwhile make
full use of market supervision so as to guarantee efficient operation of
the supervision mechanism by all means.

\(f\) Sustainability. We should enable the service providers, consumers
and regulators to achieve win-win in business activities so that the
system activities can form a closed loop and realize benign development.

#### 3.1.3 Definition of Digital Finance

The digital revolution has given rise to new forms of digital economy
and has driven the digital transformation of rural finance. Digital
finance in China can be traced back to the launch of Alipay in 2004, but
within the financial industry, 2013 when Yu\'E Bao was launched is often
considered as the starting point for the development of digital finance
in China. The digital transformation of large commercial banks and
financial technology companies conducting financial business has
provided a reference for the digitalization of rural finance. Leveraging
digital technologies such as \"big data, cloud computing, the Internet
of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence, and blockchain\" has reduced
information asymmetry between borrowers and lenders in rural finance,
eased collateral restrictions, lowered the inherent costs and risks of
rural lending. This inclusion of the previously excluded \"long-tail
population\" into the digital inclusive financial system has been
enabled by the application of digital technologies, greatly enhancing
the accessibility and convenience of rural financial services. In 2022,
China\'s Central Document No. 1 highlighted the importance of
\"promoting digital rural development\" as a key focus for advancing
rural revitalization comprehensively. In this context, accelerating the
digital transformation of rural financial institutions can help expand
the blue ocean market of agricultural and rural big data applications,
providing high-quality financial support for the development of smart
agriculture, the enhancement of rural information infrastructure, and
the improvement of farmers\' digital literacy.

From the perspective of digital finance products, they have several
characteristics:

Low Costs: Compared to traditional financial models, where individuals
need to visit financial institutions in person and provide various paper
documents, digital finance relies on digital platforms for information
provision, verification, confirmation, pricing, and transactions. This
process does not involve face-to-face interactions, which reduces
transaction costs for both the capital seekers and suppliers. These
transaction costs include the travel expenses and time spent for capital
demanders, as well as the operational costs and personnel expenses for
capital suppliers.

High Efficiency: Digital finance leverages digital advantages to make
operations more convenient and standardized. Unlike traditional finance,
which involves multi-level approvals in the credit process, often
leading to significant delays, digital finance utilizes database
technology, network information platforms, and an approval system that
can process applications in a matter of minutes. This dramatically
improves efficiency.

Wider Coverage: Traditional financial development models are relatively
limited. Despite the rapid development of the non-interest income sector
(third-party businesses) in the banking industry in recent years, and
innovations in the capital market, the core services remained relatively
unchanged. New financial products might not be widely adopted because
many customers lack an understanding of financial innovations. Digital
finance, on the other hand, addresses the issues of time and geography,
mainly focusing on addressing customers\' most pressing issues such as
loans and financial management. It also covers the service gaps in
traditional financial services, which makes it easier to promote and
improves resource allocation efficiency.

### 3.2 Theoretical Analysis

#### 3.2.1 Rural Finance Development Theory

Rural finance has been developing worldwide for many years, but there is
still no correct and standard definition of this term in the academic
community. Some scholars maintain that rural finance refers to all the
activities related to monetary circulation and credit relationship that
occur in rural areas. In this definition, rural finance can be directly
understood as finance in rural areas. But with the development of rural
finance and its theoretical studies, many acts related to rural finance
have gone beyond the scope of this concept, challenging the status of
this concept. Other scholars define the concept of rural finance from
the perspective of the results it can bring. According to them, rural
finance is not a single item but a system with complete functions and
complex structure. This complete system is not only a bridge to combine
rural economy with national finance but also a link to provide new ideas
for rural economic development. It is generally acknowledged that the
latter concept can better reflect the value of rural finance and is also
widely accepted among the scholars.

In this dissertation, we demonstrate that rural finance should be
defined from the following three aspects: firstly, from the perspective
of region, rural finance is found in rural areas, namely the finance in
administrative areas at the county level or below; secondly, regarding
its function, rural finance serves to promote rural economic
development; and thirdly, regarding the system, rural finance is the
finance developing under the socialist market economic system. As an
important part of economic development, finance is susceptible to the
changes of economic environment and times. And as an indispensable part
of finance, rural finance also gives birth to many corresponding schools
following the introduction of modern financial development theory. At
present, there are four main paradigms in the field of rural finance in
developing countries: Financial Exclusion Paradigm, Subsidized
Agricultural Credit Paradigm, Rural Financial Market Paradigm and
Incomplete Competitive Market Paradigm.

\(I\) Financial Exclusion Paradigm

Financial exclusion can be defined as the inability of some groups to
enter the financial sector and access necessary financial services in an
appropriate form. Financial exclusion was first proposed by geographers
Leyshon and Thrift et al. in the 20th century. At first, people
comprehended financial exclusion mainly from the standpoints of
geographical discrimination and financial spatial differences. Later,
the concept of financial exclusion expanded from simple geographical
analysis to wider financial discrimination. However, rural areas in
China are plagued by serious financial exclusion attributable to the
following two reasons. First of all, in terms of demand, different
households and businesses in rural areas have different demands for
financial services because of differences in financial standing and
income level as well as production and management conditions
respectively. When making a decision on whether to extend loans to rural
groups, financial institutions need to take a full consideration of the
customer's financial position and personal information, which will lead
to financial exclusion of rural groups. Secondly, in terms of supply,
rural groups in the countryside are prone to liquidity risk and moral
hazard, so financial institutions face greater risks in granting loans
to rural groups. In addition, small and scattered rural customers
increase transaction costs, which will also induce financial exclusion
of rural groups by financial institutions. Financial exclusion will hold
back the development of financial inclusion toward rural areas.

\(II\) Subsidized Agricultural Credit Paradigm

In early days, the Subsidized Agricultural Credit Paradigm represented
the authority of the rural finance theoretical perspectives, stating
that special funds of other external financial institutions must be
channeled to rural areas to promote economic development in the
countryside. According to this theory, the main problem faced by rural
residents is fund shortage, as evident by their inability to save money
and to obtain external financing with highly unstable agricultural
production. This theory implies that rural finance relies largely on the
government's adjustment efforts and support. The reason is that informal
financial institutions generally practice higher interest rates, which
will exert extremely negative impacts on the income of rural residents
and in turn widen the gap between the rich and the poor. Therefore, the
government must launch policies to crack down civil usury and directly
provide policy and legal supports to rural finance. According to the
Subsidized Agricultural Credit Paradigm, developing countries adopted
relevant rural financial policies in the past, producing special loans
targeting the poor.

\(III\) Rural Financial Market Paradigm

By the 1980s, people realized that the rural financial adjustment relied
on the government policies could no longer meet rural residents' demand
for funds, and there were many loopholes and drawbacks in the allocation
of funds, such as inefficiency. Therefore, the Rural Financial Market
Paradigm, which is completely opposite to the Subsidized Agricultural
Credit Paradigm, appeared in the spotlight. This theory strongly
supports rural financial reform, stressing that rural residents are able
to saving money and rural areas can realize financial management through
flow of their own funds, without the need of external financial support.
At the same time, this theory recognizes the act of informal financial
institutions to set high interest rates because it matches with the high
risk of rural funds. On the contrary, the low interest rate policy of
external funds not only increases residents' dependence on external
funds but also imposes a devastating negative impact on the internal
flow of rural funds. Supporters of this theory claim that rural
financial reform is imperative. In the process of reform, it is
necessary to maintain the internal operation of rural financial
behaviors, mobilize the savings of rural residents, set reasonable
interest rates, recognize the existence of informal financial
institutions without introducing them from outside, and seek for the
most favorable balance among different financial behaviors and choices.

\(IV\) Imperfect Competitive Market Paradigm

The outbreak of financial crisis in the late 21st century has forced the
whole world to reconsider the importance of government intervention to
market management and economic development. Correspondingly, the
researchers on rural finance theory also realized that we must resort to
the means of government macro-control in order to ensure sound
development of rural finance. Stiglitz's Incomplete Competitive Market
Paradigm is representative of these theories. Stiglitz claimed that
developing countries are faced by the problem of imperfect market
competition due to information asymmetry in the process of financial
transactions. And if we completely rely on the market mechanism for
adjustment, we will probably face market failure. Therefore, non-market
behaviors such as government intervention should be made a part of rural
financial reform, only that they cannot become a dominant action. While
avoiding the problems of market adjustment and government intervention,
incomplete market can not only facilitate the growth of rural finance in
an environment with a suitable inflation rate but also place rural
finance under reasonable and effective control and regulation while
securing free development.

#### 3.2.2 Theoretical Basis of Digital Finance

\(1\) Connotation of Digital Finance

Digital finance refers to the use of digital technology in financial
services, combining it with traditional financial institutions to create
a new financial behavior pattern that enables functions such as
information exchange and the flow of funds. The emergence of digital
finance, in the context of the new era of technology, has met new market
demands. It is an emerging field that extends from the combination of
digital technology industry and traditional financial industry.
Traditional financial institutions fully leverage digital technology to
integrate finance with digital, and digital finance emerges as a new
format in the financial industry. Traditional financial institutions
mainly provide substantial product solutions to the business end of
digital finance, while some digital technology support companies focus
on the development of mobile app programs for these specific product
solutions, providing online platforms for the products, and handling
product listing and promotion.

\(2\) The Theoretical Foundation of Digital Finance

In financial markets, there are many issues such as incomplete financial
information, high financial transaction costs, and irregular trading
behavior. The emergence of the digital finance model combines the
traditional physical economy model with online virtual economic
services. This not only drives the transformation of existing economic
models but also effectively addresses many issues in the financial
market. To study digital finance, one needs to consider three
fundamental aspects: financial development, financial intermediation,
and the financial function view.

1.  Financial Development

Financial development theory is based on the structure of finance theory
developed by Goldsmith and is shaped by the extensive research of other
financial scholars. It focuses on the relationship between the financial
industry and the economic development of a country. This theory
investigates questions like how financial institutions are established,
how financial systems are perfected, and how financial markets are
established. In the process of researching financial development, King
and Levine in the early 1990s were the first to design four indicators
to measure the role of finance in the development of an economy. These
four indicators are the Bank indicator (measuring the scale of
commercial banks relative to central banks), Privy indicator (similar to
Private), Depth indicator (measuring the scale of financial
intermediaries), and Private indicator (measuring bank loans to private
enterprises). By establishing fixed indicators and data parameters, they
concluded that financial development is the cause of economic
development, and the growth effect obtained from economic development is
the result. Subsequent empirical data samples have confirmed this
conclusion, indicating that if the financial industry can receive
favorable development conditions, it can not only promote productivity
but also contribute to sustainable economic growth. Research on
financial development in China emerged in the 1990s in the context of
economic globalization. Through an exploration of the relationship
between finance and the economy and the discussion of the sustainable
development concept of the two, the renowned Chinese financial scholar
Bai Qinxian first proposed the theory of sustainable financial
development. The purpose of this theory is to serve the development of
productivity by understanding the internal connections between finance
and the economy and the rational allocation of assets in the economic
development process. This theory reflects the significant role of
finance in economic development and provides a practical theoretical
basis for the establishment and improvement of China\'s socialist
economic system.

2\. Financial intermediary

Financial intermediary is an institution or individual serves as a
middleman between investors and savers in the process of capital
allocation. Financial intermediaries specifically include securities
institutions, financial advisory service companies, insurance companies,
commercial banks and so on. And these institutions can be classified by
nature into banking financial intermediaries and non-banking financial
intermediaries. Financial transaction has a long history. In early days,
financial transactions could not be closed successfully without a
financial intermediary due to the asymmetry of information between the
counterparties. Along with the gradual economic development, continuous
improvement of the relevant financial system and establishment and
expansion of the financial market, financial intermediaries in the
market of the early days started to produce a series of problems. After
the 1960s, in order to adapt to the new financial environment, Schotens
and Wensveen (1969) re-studied and re-defined the concept of financial
intermediary. They asserted that financial intermediary operating in the
new financial environment no longer served as a middleman responsible
for information perfection but as an independent participant in the
financial market. As the financial environment changed, the relationship
between financial intermediary and the evolving financial market
generated more new research directions. The present Financial
Intermediary Theory is improving gradually along with the ever-changing
financial market environment. A fairly mature research system has been
established at abroad. we should, based on the actualities of financial
market in China, make full use of these theoretical research findings to
build a modern financial system.

3\. Financial functional paradigm

Studies on traditional financial market are based on the Financial
Institutional Paradigm, that is, to analyze financial behaviors and
activities under the assumption that the setting of financial
institutions remains unchanged. This method is proven to have many
problems in the ever-changing financial market environment. Since the
establishment of financial institutions is determined by the specific
financial market environment, the types and functions of financial
institutions are constantly changing with the continuous development of
the financial market. So, in later financial studies, Bodie and Morton
(2000) put forward a new research direction, that is, to analyze and
study the changes of financial market from the perspective of financial
functions. And they summarized six core functions of finance:
fundamental function of money transfer, pooling and reallocation of
financial resources, circulation of financial resources, risk control of
financial system, information supply for financial transactions and
solving incentive problems.

\(1\) Money transfer. The function of money transfer is one of the most
fundamental functions of finance. With the changes of payment means and
currencies, the money transfer function in the new era is not only
limited to offline physical financial transactions but, more
importantly, referring to online financial transactions backed by Alipay
and other third-party payment platforms. This information-based
initiative incorporates more and more financial institutions, especially
the emerging digital financial institutions, into the financial sector
and help them to play the important role of money transfer.

\(2\) Pooling and reallocation of financial resources. Pooling and
reallocation of financial resources refers to the function of
establishing appropriate mechanisms in the process of financial
transactions to pool financial resources in the industry in a short time
and then complete operation of these financial resources in the
financial market. This function applies mainly to purchase of corporate
shares in the financial market.

\(3\) Circulation of financial resources. It refers to the rational
allocation and reuse of economic resources, which is the most essential
application of the financial system. However, the efficiency of
allocation and reuse of economic resources is often affected by the
complexity of economic transactions. In the financial framework under
the background of the present international financial system, it has
become possible to allocate, use and reuse economic resources across
countries.

\(4\) Risk control of financial system. Professional financial services
mostly deal with the risk control of economic resources, such as options
trading services. Essentially, the circulation of funds in financial
transactions will inevitably generate risks, so the establishment of the
financial system will surely provide a variety of controls for
predictable risks.

\(5\) Information supply. In the process of financial transactions, the
supply of price information can often offer some reference for the
participants of market transactions, thereby reducing the loss resulting
from the lack of information. The effective functioning of information
supply can not only provide data support for financial institutions in
risk control but also make financial transactions more transparent.

\(6\) Solving incentive problems. Incentive problems are bound to affect
other functions of the financial system and relevant financial
institutions in resource allocation, transfer of resources, risk
management, etc. The consequences, for instance, include adverse
selection between principals and agents and moral hazard. Especially
when there exists information asymmetry in the market, the financial
system can provide relevant solutions to incentive problems.

### 3.3 Interrelationships among Rural Finance, Financial Inclusion and Digital Finance

In this dissertation, we analyze the logical relationship among rural
finance, financial inclusion and digital finance, and discuss the
feasibility of developing rural inclusive finance via digitalization.

#### 3.3.1 Relation between Rural Finance and Financial Inclusion

Financial inclusion is a concept and overall framework intended to
resolve financial exclusion and eradicate financial discrimination.
Financial exclusion is the term coined for the poor and unprivileged
groups lacking access to mainstream financial services at affordable
costs. The factors underlying financial exclusion may include
geographical, economic, social and financial service market factors.
Geographical exclusion refers to the fact that the excluded subjects are
far away from financial service institutions and need public transport
to get to financial service institutions; economic exclusion means
mainstream financial institutions shut those engaged in high-risk and
low-income operations outside through industry risk assessment and
otherwise; social exclusion means mainstream financial institutions
exclude economic participants using certain marketing strategies or the
excluded subjects themselves believe that there is only a slim chance
for them to obtain financial services even if they submit applications
in view of their experience and psychological factors, think that they
are virtually excluded from financial services.

Rural areas, agriculture and farmers are the key service objects of
rural finance. Rural areas are remote and far away from financial
institutions, and long-distance transportation is needed before reaching
them, falling into the category of geographical exclusion; agriculture
is a high-risk and low-return industry, falling into the category of
economic exclusion; farmers are less educated and find it hard to afford
financial services, falling into the category of social exclusion. The
above analysis shows that the service objects of rural finance are
basically the objects of financial exclusion. The main goal of financial
inclusion is to eradicate financial exclusion, including, of course, to
solve the financial inclusion problem of "agriculture, rural areas and
farmers", that is, to solve the current problems of rural finance.
Therefore, financial inclusion provides conception and basic framework
for solving rural financial problems.

To sum up, financial inclusion is a concept targeting at solving rural
financial problems. And financial problems in underdeveloped rural areas
are an important aspect of financial inclusion.

#### 3.3.2 Relation between Rural Finance and Digital Technology

1\. Digital Integration into Finance: One of the core features of the
early digital transformation of rural financial institutions is the
digitization of financial services and business channels. The rapid
development of financial technology has driven innovation in rural
financial services, with digital technology gradually shifting from
supporting to leading these services. In the process of digital
transformation, digital technology is embedded throughout the entire
process of financial services and deeply integrated with finance. This
results in digitally-based transaction scenarios, data-driven analysis
and exploration, as well as comprehensive digital credit management
mechanisms.

2\. Finance Embedded into Digital: In the digital era of the economy,
financial competition is about financial ecosystems and data platforms.
During the process of digital transformation, finance is integrated into
various aspects of people\'s daily lives, creating a new format of
scenario-based finance, providing people with ecological scenario
financial services. Therefore, the digital transformation of rural
financial institutions is not merely the adoption of digital technology
but rather a comprehensive, mutually empowering process between the
organization and technology. As digital transformation advances, digital
technology gradually extends into the strategic planning, organizational
structure, business models, product design, operational mechanisms, and
IT systems of rural financial institutions, making digitalization a
fundamental aspect of rural finance. In the process of the mutual
embedding of digital technology and rural finance, a digital financial
ecosystem gradually takes shape.

Based on the analysis above, we can see that rural finance falls within
the important category of inclusive finance, which can help improve the
underdeveloped state of rural finance. However, the specific approach
requires further discussion. The application of digitalization is one of
the practical pathways for inclusive finance and has already proven its
inclusivity in urban financial development. Whether digitalization can
solve rural financial issues becomes a crucial question. Logically, it
is feasible, primarily for the following reasons:

The fundamental difference between rural and urban areas lies in
\"agriculture, rural areas, and farmers.\" Digital technology can
effectively accommodate this difference. It can aggregate small,
dispersed rural areas online, consolidate and analyze data and
information that is difficult to collect, and create a \"small world\"
on digital platforms that is no different from urban areas.

Agriculture represents a significant difference. The application of
digitalization in urban finance has demonstrated its effectiveness for
the \"long tail\" market, and agriculture falls into this category,
making it compatible with digital solutions.

Farmer differences: Digital finance not only brings financial services
but also leads farmers to enjoy the benefits of information technology,
broadening their horizons and improving their overall financial
literacy.

In summary, digital applications can, in macrological terms, enhance the
inclusiveness of rural finance.

### 3.4 Inevitability for the Digitalization Entry into Rural Financial Market

#### 3.4.1 Theoretical Basis for the Digital Technology's Role in Promoting Rural Financial Inclusion

Branchless banking is an important means to achieve financial inclusion.
Branchless banking is defined as the strategy of delivering financial
services outside conventional bank branches. On the one hand, this
strategy may be complementary to the functions of existing banking
branches, and collaboration between the two may broaden the channel of
financial services; on the other hand, branchless banking may exist as a
channel independent from conventional banking branches, completely
replacing and getting rid of the mode of delivering financial services
based on physical branches. Branchless banking technologies include the
digital banking, ATM, POS, EFTPOS, mobile banking and so on. A survey of
2011 showed that 62% of respondents preferred the digital banking as
their bank service channel, while only 20% chose the traditional mode of
banking outlet service (in 2007, 40% preferred the mode of banking
outlet service) (PwC, 2012). Major reasons behind that include the
improvement of customer digitalization and the increase of
infrastructure cost for physical branches.

Application of digital information technology or information and
communication technology has become a very important contributor of
financial inclusion (Gupta, 2011). In recent years, many developing
countries have made use of information and communication technologies
(ICTs) to innovate the means and forms of financial service delivery,
effectively expanding the reach of financial services to remote and poor
people and increasing the accessibility of financial services to
unprivileged groups. The concept and models of e-financial Inclusion
were introduced in such context. ICT-based financial innovation can
reduce the cost and improve the convenience of access to financial
services. Mobile finance plays an increasingly prominent role in
accelerating financial inclusion and economic growth. For example, a
study on African countries was conducted using the data of 1988-2007 to
analyze the impact of mobile phone development on financial inclusion
and economic growth. The results showed that mobile phone development
imposes a significant positive impact on economic growth because, among
other reasons, the mobile phone development facilitates financial
inclusion (Andrianaivo and Kpodar, 2012). Another study reported that in
Colombia, a South American country with underdeveloped finance, the
government and the private sector have developed many channels in an
attempt to reduce the transaction cost of financial services, improve
the availability of financial services and achieve financial inclusion,
including financial services using the Internet, voice mail, mobile
phones, bank agents, etc. An important means is to facilitate delivery
of financial services using mobile banking (Fernndez de Lis, 2014).

A report published by Dun & Bradstreet in 2014 pointed out that
increased use of the Internet in financial services would help increase
transparency and reduce distribution costs. According to the data of D&B
survey, 84% of the respondents preferred the Internet as the main
channel of financial service delivery. The Internet helps to improve the
accessibility of financial services (expanding coverage), enhance
operational efficiency and reduce costs. The impacts of the Internet on
financial inclusion are reflected in two aspects. The first is the
transparency of product prices and features (product publicity and
presentation). Second, the use of the digital technology can increase
market segmentation and product segregation so that financial service
providers can design different products for different customer groups,
thereby reducing distribution and delivery costs.

In fact, some large credit rating agencies have become increasingly
concerned with the problem of how to apply digital technologies in
mining of rural big data so as to provide credit rating services for
rural lending activities.

For example, the Policy and Economic Research Council (PERC) of the
United States and Experian MicroAnalytics (EMA) worked together to
develop the Financial Identity Risk Management (FIRM), an innovative
scheme for improving the accessibility of financial services in emerging
markets (especially the rural poor populations), in a hope to improve
the data needed for credit risk assessment and borrowing capacity
appraisal.

The Issue 76 (Feb. 2014) of *ICT Update*, an E-journal focusing on ICTs,
agriculture and rural development in Africa, the Caribbean and the
Pacific, set the topic as mobile finance and agriculture, stating that
mobile finance would revolutionize the finance of the agricultural
supply chain (ICT Update, 2014). An article titled "Big data for small
holder" was devoted to introducing FIRM.

The development of digital technology and the improvement of rural
financial informatization can reduce the cost of information collection
in financial services, alleviate the problem of information asymmetry,
and facilitate the delivery of credit services. The advancement of
information technology and its application in financial innovation can
reduce the operational costs of financial services delivered by banks
and enable the banks to increase services, especially in remote areas.
ICTs have a wide range of applications in financial innovation. We can
examine and comprehend the impact of information technology on financial
innovation in developing countries and relevant rationales from the
aspects of credit system construction, branchless banking, innovation of
payment means and improvement of payment environment. The rationales for
CITs to improve rural financial service innovation and financial supply
and to promote financial service innovation and expand service coverage
in remote areas mainly includes the following aspects.

Firstly, the development of digital technology can facilitate the
establishment and improvement of the credit information system, improve
the information environment for credit decision-making of rural
financial institutions, and enhance the efficiency of credit
decision-making. Meanwhile, financial institutions can adopt information
technology to identify, measure and control credit risks and improve
risk management capabilities. Secondly, IT-based development of
financial products such as e-finance, digital finance and mobile banking
is conducive to expanding the service coverage in geographically remote
rural areas and promoting rural financial inclusion. Thirdly, financial
institutions can use information technology to improve the information
system and enhance internal management efficiency, especially the
vertical management ability, so as to facilitate sinking of service
delivery channels. Fourthly, financial development backed by electronic
information technology can cement rural financial infrastructure,
improve the construction of rural payment system and payment
environment, and facilitate the delivery of rural financial services.

In addition, the digital technology can not only facilitate financial
services but also further increase the sources of funding in rural
markets. At present, financing in the countryside of China mainly relies
on endogenous financing channels. That means, capitals are accumulated
from inside. But this financing channel has great shortcomings,
seriously impeding the expanded reproduction of rural economy. The
acceleration of agricultural modernization poses stricter requirements
for the efficiency and quality of agricultural production. With the
constant development and upgrading of science and technology, outdated
production equipment and technologies have been replaced, and new
equipment and technologies have been used in agricultural production.
However, the shortage of funding for introducing these new equipment and
technologies has become a problem demanding prompt solution in rural
development. Since the reform and opening-up, China's rural land
circulation and urbanization have been advancing steadily, and the
government's support and guiding policies such as "Bringing
Culture/Science and Technology/Hygienic Health to the Countryside" and
"college-graduate village officials" have greatly improved the overall
quality of farmers. Agricultural modernization is speeding up and rural
economy shows huge potentials, leading to enormous financing demands.
All these factors will create golden opportunities for the development
of rural financial market.

Digitalization provides the rural financial market with a financing
channel featuring a relatively low threshold, convenient and fast
services and relatively fair and equal financing opportunities.
Innovative crowdfunding, P2P lending and other credit models also
increase the sources of capital in the rural financial market. Digital
finance not only plays a positive role in alleviating financing
difficulties in rural areas but also has a profound impact on driving
the modernization of agricultural industry. It leverages the remarkable
advantages of third-party payment platforms of electronic commerce to
speed up circulation of agricultural products. The Chinese government
strongly advocates the digital finance business model, which will
further the connection between rural digital finance and rural economy
and make digital finance a powerful engine driving the rural economic
development.

#### 3.4.2 Cases Illustrating the Digital Technology's Role in Promoting Rural Financial Inclusion

\(I\) African cases: M-PESA in Kenya and MTN in Uganda

In Africa, most of the population have no access to financial services.
Only 20% of African households have bank accounts. For example, in
Ethiopia, Uganda and Tanzania, a bank offers financial services to
100,000 people on average. Moreover, even those who have access to the
banking services often complaint about their inconvenience in view of
high transaction costs (Ondiege, 2010). Facing this situation, African
countries have been alleviating financial exclusion by developing mobile
banking, mobile wallet and other products and services in the past few
years. Most notably, Kenya-based Safaricom introduced mobile banking and
POS terminal services to successfully launch the M-PESA service program.
The target customers of M-PESA are those having no access to banking
services and no bank accounts. Also, M-PESA was introduced to Tanzania
by Vodacom.

In Uganda, MTN introduced the card-based mobile wallet. The service is
to help customers with deposit operations through 1,300 agents.
Customers will receive text messages prompting that the deposit has been
posted, and then they can transfer the money to other mobile banking
users.

\(II\) Asian cases: True Move in Thailand

In Thailand, the mobile operator True has contributed to the development
of mobile banking. Bank of Thailand supports the use of mobile money
considering that the management cost of cash is too high. Those having
no access to banking services in Thailand are mostly unregistered
workers and refugees in shelters. Mobile money is mostly used in mobile
recharge, fund transfer, bill payment and purchase of goods, but most of
the users are young people. Thanks to the development of mobile banking,
Thailand's financial services have been increasing substantially in
recent years.

\(III\) Latin American cases: Tigo and Personal in Paraguay

Most Latin American countries are facing the problems of scattered
banking outlets and inadequate financial infrastructure. Paraguay, for
example, is one of the poorest countries in Latin America, with serious
financial inequality and insufficient access to education and financial
services in rural areas. Bank branches are most sparsely distributed in
Paraguay among all other countries in Central and South America, with
only five branches serving 100,000 people on average. Even counting some
informal financial institutions, 70% of Paraguay's population is still
excluded from financial services. However, the penetration of mobile
phones in Latin America has hit 100%. In recent years, innovations in
digital technology have increased the access to financial services. Tigo
and Personal, Latin America's two largest telecom operators, are
promoting the development of mobile wallet platforms. In Paraguay, Tigo
secures 54.67% shares of the mobile phone market. In 2008, Tigo was the
first to introduce the mobile wallet Tigo Cash that is mainly used for
retail payment. The registration is done by verifying with the agent
outlets, and the transaction is handled by transferring money from
E-wallets. In 2010, Tigo developed and launched Giros Tigo to deliver
Chinese transfer services. Customer A remits mobile money at a
designated branch, and then customer B who has received a text message
prompt goes to the branch to collect money with his PIN code. Personal,
Paraguay's second largest telecom operator, maintains 29.68% shares of
the mobile phone market. In 2010, it began to introduce the mobile
wallet Billetera Personal which is mainly used for money transfer,
commercial payment and bill payment. It has provided services for more
than one basic account.

### 4.1 Major Experience of Brazil in Development of Rural Finance 

With respect to the development of digital finance services, Brazil is
mainly characterized in its new mode that no point-to-point bank outlet
is set up. This is a brand-new development concept. How to understand
this mode? The mode will be adopted for areas with scattered agency
stores to provide business services for areas not covered by bank
outlets or areas difficult in accessing to relevant services. This
measure had been promoted and implemented extensively since the year of
2000. Up to now, great achievement has been made with this effort. The
mode is still under implementation, and supporting services for the mode
are also improved, which pushes forwarding the development of the
industry.

\(I\) Operation mode of banking business without bank outlet

With respect to banking business without bank outlet, financial services
will be provided for specific groups by retail agency stores together
with financial institutions relevant to banks. The whole process of the
business will be completed by the retail agency while banks, as the last
link of the business process, are mainly responsible for providing
sufficient fund. In this process, Caixa Aqui is the bank to provide fund
for target clients through the agency. For accessing relevant business
services of agency outlets of Caixa and relevant departments or
institutions, clients only have to provide certification documents like
generation II ID cards and residence certificates for the whole process
from the application to the use of such services.

Being popular in Brazil, this new mode is promoted quickly. As of 2007,
many subjects of rural finance have been benefited from the mode. Brazil
has promulgated relevant laws and regulations for strengthening the
supervision on the mode, which facilitates the evasion of risks in the
daily operation of the mode. For instance, relevant law specifies that
the central bank may exam the whole process of the bank in retailer
selection. If any problem is found in the process, the bank will be held
liable for the consequence. According to relevant laws, the central bank
also has the right to inspect detailed records of transactions of
institutions under the supervision of the bank. To a great extent, this
is helpful for eliminating or dissolving relevant difficulties and risks
in the early period.

\(II\) Features of banking business without outlet

Banking business without outlet are characterized as follows: With the
business, needs of target clients will be judged and catered for
precisely through credit cards and e-banks installed on mobile phones.
In this way, clients may complete the transaction without having to go
outside or being restricted by the distance. The business may also allow
third parties to provide financial services as agencies. For example,
small retailers and many other subjects may also entrust relevant
professional agencies to handle business on their behalf when target
clients are unable to handle business on site, which is a problem
difficult to solve in the past.

In addition to the foresaid functional services, clients may also have
access to regular services like deposit and withdrawal. Payment-related
business relevant to payment is the central task and function of the
business mode. Of course, basic functions like deposit business is
included. However, other functions are seldom reflected in the mode.
According to the in-depth analysis of relevant features, the mode has
great potential in the development. To a great extent, the mode will
make up deficiencies of and support existing bank outlets and cut down
the operation cost of the bank outlets.

### 4.2 Major Experience of the USA in Development of Rural Finance

The USA is outstanding in the development of ITFIN industry. Currently,
a complete set of ITFIN market has been formed in rural area of the USA.
The success of the USA in this aspect is an example for worldwide
financial organizations and institutions to learn. Rural bank is the
representative in the ITFIN market of the USA, and provides loan,
insurance, transfer and deposit services for the impoverished group in
rural areas, which are target clients of rural banks.

\(I\) Operation mode

Rural banks of the USA function in this way: With a prerequisite that
the loan amount is safe and sustainable, loan will be granted to a group
and group members will monitor each other and reallocate the resources,
thus benefiting the whole group. Specifically, five or more impoverished
persons may form a group voluntarily; the group members have equivalent
economic conditions; the bank will exam credit of potential group
members; if the potential group members passing the examination of the
bank may be admitted to the interest group and have access to loan. The
period of the examination is one week. Group members do not have to
provide guarantee or collateral. The process is as follows: Loan will be
granted to two impoverished persons in the group. If they are able to
repay the loan within specified time limit, loan will be granted to
other members of the group. This is helpful for group members to monitor
each other. The maturity period of the loan is one year. Borrowers may
repay the loan in installments and need to repay one percentage point of
the loan in each month and pay off the principal in a year. The process
will be maintained to cast off poverty of the impoverished group.

\(II\) Major features

One of features of rural banks of the USA is that loan interest rates
are differentiated based on the purpose of the loan. Loans used for
different purpose are relevant to different interest rate. Moreover, no
interest will accrued for loan for especially poverty-stricken rural
group. The fact shows that most farmers will choose petty loan with low
interest rate. The second feature is that high deposit interest rate
mechanism is promoted by rural banks of the USA. The rural banks use
this measure to attract deposit and strength the lending ability, thus
facilitating sustainable development of rural banks.

In addition, rural banks of the USA is distinctively featured in their
compulsive deposit policies. As a prerequisite for obtaining loan, a
borrower needs to deposit certain amount in the bank within the time
limit specified by the bank. The deposit will be considered as the fund
for forming a group. Meanwhile, the bank will offer relatively high
interest rate for the deposit. After the review of the rural bank, the
borrower may obtain loan from the bank on condition that five percent of
the raised money should be deposited into the group fund. According to
relevant investigation, up to half of the capital of rural banks of the
USA is sourced from borrowers' deposit in commercial banks. Therefore,
compulsive deposit has great function on rural banks of the USA as it
reduces the number of borrowers, relieves the problem of the interest
rate for the repayment and lowers the risk faced by rural banks of the
USA.

### 4.3 Major Experience of Japan in Development of Rural Finance

BRI is one of the largest state-owned commercial financial institutions
in the world to provide financial services for rural areas while Japan
has the largest client group for the bank. BRI provides financial
services for most rural families and carries out other businesses
smoothly in Japan.

ITFIN mainly caters for families with low family economic income and
economic difficulties. As it is expensive to provide financial services
for families with economic difficulties and it is often hard for banks
providing such services for such targets to recover the capital, the
subsidiary of the government and the country becomes the only support
for relevant banks. Consequently, many banking institutions are not
willing to provide financial services and always try to avoid the
financial services for those rural families for preventing capital loss.
However, Japan solve this problem in a good way: Internet-based petty
loan mode and financial services for impoverished families are combined,
improved and innovated into a status that the provision of such
financial services has no adverse impact on banks. In the end, BRI
functions smoothly and makes great achievement in the development of
financial services in Japan.

\(1\) Operation mode of BRI

The supreme banking department in Japan is BRI Credit Department.
Established in 1895, BRI has a history of over 100 years. It is one of
major state-owned commercial banks of Indonesia. Business of BRI is
mainly divided into three parts: commercial finance and petty loan
finance. Petty credit functioning in commercial principles is the core
business of rural bank system of BRI while ordinary agriculture-related
loan developed from policy-based subsidiary loan is the major business
of the system.

BRI has institutions of three levels: branch, sub-branch and rural bank.
Rural bank falls in the bottom layer of the operation mode of BRI. BRI
Credit Department is the dominant department which directs, controls and
makes decisions for the credit business. In the operation mode of BRI,
BRI Credit Department formulates credit business policies and manages
departments of lower levels; each department needs to monitor and manage
its subsidiary department and audit the calculation work. Up to now, BRI
has set up 324 sub-branches in cities of Japan. These sub-branches are
distributed in major cities mainly for carrying out credit business
targeting individuals or collectives, monitoring and reviewing credit
business of rural banks, and making decisions on the upper limit of loan
amount together with rural banks. Rural banks of all levels must bear
the expense accrued in the process of credit business. According to
statistics, the number of rural banks in Japan has been up to 4046 and
keeps going up. These rural banks are distributed in places of Japan
under the decision based on BRI mode.

As the core of internet petty loan, rural banks undertake the work of
independent financial accounting and examination. Generally, rural banks
are set up in urban areas and towns or places near to markets. To reduce
operation cost and expenditure, rural banks would recruit employees in
local places. This will simplify expenditure and internal structure of
banks, reduce expenditure of banks, enhance the communication of banks
with local residents, reduce local resources consumed by human resources
of banks, and relieve moral risk faced by banks. In addition to the
reduction of human resource cost, recruiting personnel in local places
will make loan audit simple and convenient, cut down loan service
expense, help to evade the risk arising from inaccurate information, and
strengthen comprehensive power and competitiveness of the banks.

\(II\) Major features

Banks under BRI operation mode will offer credit services which allows
for installments, which relieve the economic difficulties of relevant
families. Loan for operation has a period of maturity of about 24 month,
loan for investment has a period of maturity of about 36 months, and
individuals or units only have the repay the loan on monthly basis.
Borrowers may also repay the loan in lump sum. Generally, the period of
repayment may be extended for about 9 months. To prevent borrowers from
failing in timely repayment because of shortage in fund or for other
reasons, banks will deduct monthly 10% of the interest from the loan to
help borrowers to repay the loan. The method of repayment with both
interest and principal is adopted by the banks based on the credit risk
theory and cash flow theory. In this way, the balance between working
capital and financial revenue and expenditure of the banks will be
maintained; and the risk faced by the banks in the provision of loan
services will be also reduced.

Before releasing the loan, the bank will exam the borrower to determine
whether the borrower is able to repay the loan on schedule. After the
borrowed is deemed capable of repayment on schedule, the loan will then
be approved and released. Moreover, staff of the bank should learn about
status of the borrower periodically to lower the risk in credit
services.

### 4.4 Experiences of Western Countries and Major Enlightenment

#### 4.4.1 Control Risk by the Adoption of Group Joint Guarantee Mode

Group joint guarantee mode has substitute the traditional relation
between the borrower and the lender and the relation between financial
unit and the borrower. To a certain extent, the mode lowers the credit
risk caused by asymmetric information and also helps individuals or
collectives with economic difficulties. Joint guarantee mode also
reduces the risk in credit finance. For example, under the joint
guarantee mode adopted in the USA, individuals or collectives will
obtain loan under the five-group joint guarantee mode, which effectively
reduces the risk in loan activities and maintains the repayment rate of
over 98%.

#### 4.4.2 Stick to Marketized Operation Mechanism

Rural finance market will be developed stably only when rural finance
follows the law of market. Government's assistance and support for rural
finance is limited. Therefore, rural finance needs to be developed in a
marketized and commercial mode. Digital finance cost should be covered
via interest rate pricing based on market condition. Sufficient capital
sources and healthy development of financial enterprises will be
maintained only when Digital finance functions through the market. Major
targets of credit business in of rural finance are individuals or
families with economic difficulties in rural areas. The target group is
characterized in the low amount of loan and extensive distribution,
which greatly pushes up the labor cost and expenditure of loan
activities in daily work. This is also the cause of the high interest
rate. Only high interest rate may sustain the daily expenditure and
maintain normal operation of the financial institution. Interest rate of
rural banks of the USA is often higher than other banks, which is a key
factor for the success of those rural banks. According to statistical
data, annual interest rate of rural banks of the USA is about 20% while
that of Japan even reaches up to 40%. High annual interest rate is the
only way to maintain normal operation, balance of revenue and
expenditure and development of financial institutions,

#### 4.4.2 Reduce Cost through Advanced Technologies

Banking business without outlets may be promoted not only in
metropolises but also in rural areas. A bank may invest certain amount
of fund in the rural area to establish relevant business, complete
facilities, reduce the expense for setting up business without outlets,
and cut down unnecessary expenditure. We can see from the case of Brazil
that the cost of setting up POS machine in areas without outlets is less
than USD 3000, and the monthly cost is only USD 160. In contrast, the
construction of a physical bank operation premise will cost USD 400,000
and the expense for personnel training and equipment is also high.
Therefore, setting up agencies without outlets is much cheaper than the
construction of physical operation premise. With this aspect, China may
also set up agencies without outlets, reduce operation costs and improve
operation efficiency of banks by borrowing the mode of Brazil.

\(1\) Establish a complete regulation mechanism. Different aspects

\(2\) Strengthen human resource construction. In modern society, talent

development of rural financial industry has its own features and the

\(3\) Perfect reward and punishment mechanism. It is very important for

### 5.1 Current Situation of Development of Rural Finance in China

#### 5.1.1 Moderate-scale Operation Induces Demand for Rural Finance

Rural labor has undergone significant migration, and new agricultural
operators continue to emerge, with land transfer and operational scale
development becoming an inevitable trend. According to statistics from
the Ministry of Agriculture, as of the latest publicly available data in
2020, the total area of contracted arable land transferred reached 530
million mu, exceeding one-third of the total area of contracted arable
land. In some eastern coastal areas, the transfer ratio has already
exceeded one-half. The number of large-scale farming households with
operational arable land exceeding 50 mu has exceeded 3.5 million,
covering an area of over 350 million mu. The number of new entities such
as family farms, farmer cooperatives, and leading agricultural
industrial enterprises has exceeded 2.7 million. As of 2020, there were
2.22 million registered farmer cooperatives nationwide, and the number
of farmers joining these cooperatives accounted for nearly 50% of the
total number of farmers in the country.

\(I\) "Three-right" split policy for land to guide moderate-scale
development of agriculture

In the early stage of reform and opening, "two-power" split policy
incited the activeness of rural residents. Agricultural productivity is
low and food is the most pressing matter at the time. With a
prerequisite that rural land is owned collectively, the right of land
management was contracted to rural residents, and the household contract
responsibility system with remuneration linked to output was
implemented.

In 2008, the process of confirming rural contracted management rights
was initiated, and through the \"confirmation of rights\" and the
issuance of land certificates, the actual rights of land contracting
were determined. Following the principles of clear ownership, complete
rights, smooth transfer, and strict protection in the property rights
system, the central government then deployed the registration and
certification of rural collective land ownership and rural residents\'
contracted management rights. In 2013, the Central Committee issued its
first directive, outlining the comprehensive registration and
confirmation of rural land rights. In 2016, the pilot program was
expanded to include 22 provinces. As of October 2016, 2,545 counties
(cities, districts), 29,000 townships, and 492,000 villages had carried
out this process, covering a total area of 750 million mu, which is
nearly 60% of the total area of family-contracted arable land.

With the advancement of urbanization, a significant number of rural
laborers have migrated to urban areas for employment in the secondary
and tertiary industries. Many rural residents have transferred their
land to others for management. To meet the trend of developing modern
agriculture and the need for rural residents to retain contracted land
rights while willing to transfer management rights, the \"Opinions on
Improving the Separation of Ownership, Contract, and Management Rights
of Rural Land\" was introduced. This policy currently implements the
parallel system of collective land ownership, rural residents\'
contracted land rights, and land management rights, known as the
\"separation of the three rights.\" This system upholds collective land
ownership, stabilizes the contracted land rights of rural residents, and
revitalizes land management rights. It lays the institutional foundation
for guiding the orderly transfer of land management rights, promoting
moderately scaled agriculture, and driving the development of modern
agriculture.

#### 5.1.2 Rural Land Reform Creates New Market for Agricultural Financial Service

In recent years, China has been pursuing a path of rural financial
innovation that extends the urban financial system into rural areas. As
of the end of the second quarter of 2023, the balance of loans related
to agriculture in financial institutions amounted to 54.6 trillion yuan,
with rural loans in China reaching 45.6 trillion yuan, both of which
exhibited a year-on-year growth rate of approximately 16%. However,
despite these significant figures, they still cannot fully meet the
growing demand for agricultural financial services in the face of the
\"new trends\" and \"new normal\" characterized by large-scale,
intensive, specialized, and modernized agricultural development in
China. Moreover, due to the underdeveloped economy and extreme income
instability among those engaged in agriculture, coupled with
deficiencies in the traditional agricultural financial service system,
it has had a negative impact on the enthusiasm of enterprises to engage
in agricultural finance. It also limits the opportunities for those in
the agricultural sector to access financial funds and services.
Therefore, there is a need for the expansion and innovation of the
existing agricultural financial service system to address these
challenges and better serve the evolving needs of the agricultural
sector.

According to *Several Opinions on Fully Deepening Rural Reform and
Accelerating Agricultural Modernization* (No.1 Document issued by the
State Council in 2014), "land right confirmation should be accelerated
and transfer of production resources to scale operation subjects should
be promoted through land circulation under the free will be rural
residents; moderate-scale agricultural operation and cultivation of
emerging operation subjects like rural residents and agricultural
cooperatives should be encouraged". The enhancement of scale and
intensive operation pushes up the agricultural industrialization level,
increases the demand of agricultural workers for operation fund (such as
increase in subsidiary for purchasing agricultural machineries with the
improvement of agricultural mechanization level), and leads to constant
growth of fund for agricultural technology development. It is
particularly worth noting that policies published since 2014 have
endowed the power and function of capital to land contracting right and
management right, pilot mortgage loan with land contracting right and
management right as collateral and pilot land trust in the northeast
China and Anhui Province have been conducted, which creates a brand-new
market for agricultural financial services. Capitalization of land
contracting right and management right solve the problem that financial
support for rural areas is not matched with current situation of rural
scale operation due to lack of mortgage guarantee. According to the
estimation of experts of the Ministry of Agriculture, the scale of loan
for land circulation will be up to RMB 1 trillion.

special financial bonds for "agriculture, rural area and farmer" based
on the effective increase in loan amount to push the agriculture-related
loan growth rate up to level which is higher than the average growth

financial innovation, the Notice points out as follows: "Guide financial

farmer's house and property right as collateral; expand the scale of

institutions in the banking industry to draw up agricultural credit
plan, delegate loan approval power, simplify business decision-making
process, innovate service mode and strengthen service function."

### 5.2 China's Dilemma in Development of Rural Finance

China are relatively weak, which is mainly reflected in following
aspects:

\(2\) Weak foundation of rural financial services

In the process of long-term development, rural area has weak foundation
for rural financial services due to the relatively poor development
foundation and natural environment, incomplete policy-based supporting
system for the development of rural finance and imperfect risk system
construction. The situation is reflected as follows: Postal Savings Bank
of China (PSBC) have set up service outlets in rural areas in China.
However, the loan capability of PSBC is limited. Commercial financial
institutions (such as the Agricultural Bank of China and Industrial and
Commercial Bank of China) which entered in the industry in the beginning
of the development of rural finance have started to withdraw from the
market of the industry. Rural credit cooperative is the major force for
the development of rural finance. However, there are many problems in
the development of rural credit cooperative, such as weak foundation,
large amount of debt and poor risk-preventing ability. To a great
extent, the problems affect the function of rural credit cooperative in
promoting the development of rural finance. In addition, some rural
credit cooperatives excellent in operation performance accept the reform
actively in the tide of marketization reform and tend to be separated
from agriculture. In these years, a number of emerging rural financial
institutions are developed with the original intention of making up the
vacancy of development of rural finance. However, the short development
time and problematic development strategic and product positioning make
the number of outlets and overall development scale unsatisfactory.
There are also objective restrictions in regional factor. Consequently,
these institutions did not make any substantial change to the structure
of rural finance. Rural residents and small and medium-sized enterprises
are faced with a narrow financing channel and high financing cost owing
to the development situation of rural finance with weak service
foundation. Consequently, these groups are forced to choose
privately-owned financial institutions, which greatly reduces the credit
availability.

#### 5.2.2 Deficiency in Information Channel of Rural Financial Market

Rural areas are often relatively backward in economic development,
scattered in distribution, and lagging behind in system binding
construction and information channel construction, which tends to cause
the situation of information asymmetry. Information asymmetry is common
in the development of rural financial development, which directly makes
it difficult for rural financial institutions and financial institution
relevant to agriculture to truly understand effective demand of rural
residents and agricultural enterprises and analyze their demand for loan
through mastering the credit status and enterprise operation condition
of these groups. Consequently, the credit threshold is raised up, which
affects the rationality of credit allocation. In addition, loan flows in
various ways and loan capital purposes vary from one another, thus
making it more difficult for relevant departments of rural financial
institutions in monitoring, increasing the monitoring cost and, to
certain extent, pushing up the probability of moral risk of the credit
market. Therefore, rural financial institutions have to be always
prudent. Loan screening effect caused by information asymmetry will
affect market clearing and credit allocation. In this circumstance,
low-income group will bear not only the pain of low-value consumption
caused by lack of assets but also the tiredness caused by low income. As
a result, some rural residents in rural areas will be rejected from
accessing credit services from financial institutions, which is
unfavorable for healthy development of rural finance. In the process of
rural finance system reform in these years, China has strengthened the
efforts in making up the deficiency in information channels, such as
through the setting up of village and town banks. However, the effect of
the efforts needs to be observed in the future.

#### 5.2.4 "Difficulties" of Borrower and Lender in Rural Finance"

Rural financial institutions, rural residents and small and medium-sized
enterprises are subjects of rural financial activities. Major content of
rural financial activities is loan. However, the fact in China in
current stage is that "rural residents are confronted with difficulty in
obtaining loan while rural financial institutions are faced with
difficulty in lending", which greatly affects the effect of function of
rural financial institutions and impedes the development of rural
economy.

\(I\) Difficulty of rural residents in obtaining loan

\(1\) Difficulty of ordinary rural residents in obtaining loan

For instance, target borrowers of rural credit cooperatives are ordinary
rural residents which are characterized in large number, scattered
distribution and relatively small farming scale. However, system
construction and process optimization of rural credit cooperative is not
perfect, and the loan process is relatively complicated. Therefore, it
is hard for an ordinary rural resident to receive an affirmative reply
in a timely manner after the loan application is submitted to a rural
credit cooperative. In this circumstance, ordinary rural residents often
turn to their relatives and friends for borrowing money or just reduce
the input intentionally in the process of farming, which will form a
vicious circle. In spite of the small scale of individual ordinary rural
resident, total number of ordinary rural residents is huge. If the
situation remains unchanged, agricultural production will be greatly
affected, which will not only lead to low agricultural production
efficiency but also affect the agricultural development strategy of
China.

\(2\) Difficulty of large agricultural operators in obtaining loan

In this paper, large agricultural operator means a professional operator
engaged in certain agricultural sector and operating the business in
certain scale. Different from ordinary rural residents, large
agricultural operators carry out the production in the way of enterprise
operation and make efforts to realize modernized operation and
management in the process of operation. In this process, however, they
will encounter the most serious and realistic problem that they are
short of fund. In the beginning, the government assists large
agricultural operators in expanding the production and planting scale
through preferential policies and other forms in order to stimulate
activeness of these large agricultural operators in the development.
However, these policies are not implemented constantly. The problem of
fund shortage remains unsolved. The call for "lending" by rural
financial institutions is often answered in the beginning and neglected
in the end. The difficulty of large agricultural operators in obtaining
loan is not eliminated substantially.

#### 5.2.5 Deviation in Credit Flow Direction of Rural Financial Institutions

The central government requires to "enhance the duties of financial
institutions for serving 'agriculture, rural area and farmer'",
"strengthen the service ability of commercial finance for 'agriculture,
rural area and farmer' and county-level small and micro enterprises,
enhance business power delegation to county-level branch institutions,
increase the deposit-loan rate and the proportion of agriculture-related
loan, and include the offer of agricultural credit into policy-oriented
effect evaluation and comprehensive assessment system". Requirements of
the central government show that rural financial institutions and
financial institutions relevant to agriculture need to support the
development of "agriculture, rural area and farmer" by formulating
support policies and designing specific financial products to respond to
requirements of the government. The core task of rural financial
institutions and financial institutions relevant to agriculture is to
solve fund problems encountered in the process of development of
"agriculture, rural area and farmer" by expanding the scale of
agriculture-related loan and increasing the proportion of
agriculture-related loan through the leverage of their functions, thus
pushing forward the healthy development of "agriculture, rural area and
farmer" in China. However, the situation is not attached with enough
importance, resulting in the deviation in credit flow direction of rural
financial institutions.

\(I\) Attach great importance to non-agricultural undertaking and little
importance to "agriculture, rural area and farmer"

In the market economic environment, many rural financial institutions
will not only actively evade the risk in credit but also evade business
in connection with "agriculture, rural area and farmer" with relatively
high risk in their setting of credit flow direction to realize the
commercial operation benefit. The conduct of these rural financial
institutions leads to serious deviation in credit flow direction. Some
rural financial institutions which mainly engaged in business supporting
"agriculture, rural area and farmer" deviates from the orientation of
main business to chase commercial benefit in the marketized environment.
For example, a rural credit cooperative association in a town which has
the major function of providing fund and policy-based support for the
development of "agriculture, rural area and farmer" in the area planned
to release loan of RMB 310 million in the year. However, 48% of the loan
was released to industrial enterprises. The proportion indicates that
rural financial institutions attach great importance to non-agricultural
undertaking and little importance to "agriculture, rural area and
farmer" and deviate seriously from the operation objective of the
institutions. Owing to the attraction of more commercial benefit, the
proportion may keep going up in the future, which seriously deviates
from the aim of "rural credit cooperatives", as mentioned by the central
government, to "stick to serving farmers, agriculture and rural
economy". The situation calls for the attention of the whole society.
Generally, agriculture-related loan includes loan for agriculture,
forestry industry and fishing industry, loan for circulation of

\(II\) Unclear important point

Key targets to be supported and assisted by rural financial institutions
are not defined in the process of development, which is one of the
reasons for the deviation in credit flow direction. Since agricultural
economy was developed, production and operation of farmers have
witnessed great changes. For example, a household with a farm area of 5
mu is a rural resident while a household with a farm area of 500 mu is
also listed as a rural resident. The two rural residents are not only
different in farming scale. Most importantly, production and operation
modes and the demand for fund are substantially different. Under the
background of development of agricultural economy with great efforts,
rural resident of the latter should be considered as key target to
support and assist in the development of rural finance. As this is a
trend of development, the country should implement
agriculture-supporting policies through rural finance instead of
adopting existing supporting measure.

To push forward the development of "agriculture, rural area and famer"
with rural finance, national and local departments have published a
number of policies, rules and opinions which are mainly about the
constant strengthening of support for agricultural economic
organizations. However, the actual effect of implementation indicates
that China has not yet formulated or published relatively clear and
feasible implementation measures. In other words, the problem of
"lacking keys and being unclear" in the process of formulating policies
and measures relevant to rural finance is still unsolved.

#### 5.2.6 Financial Institutions of Multiple Levels not Formed into Agriculture-Supporting Compound Force

In these years, China has been devoted to the construction of a rural
financial institution system with complete functions and clear layer and
structure and encouraging policy-based banks, commercial banks and new
rural financial institutions to join in the construction. In the process
of the construction, actual situation of development of rural finance,
especially the situation of demand and supply, should be taken into
consideration, and more financial institutions, especially rural
financial institutions and financial institutions engaged in
agriculture-related business, should be encouraged to join in the
efforts. To construct such a multi-layer rural financial system, efforts
must be made in two aspects: Firstly, medium-sized and large banks
participating into the construction should play a leading role, design
financial products and services relevant to agricultural development and
establish a perfect service mechanism. Secondly, it is necessary to
attract new rural financial institutions to participate into the
construction to enhance the competition among financial institutions in
the system and drive rural financial institutions to strengthen internal
construction, perfect their products and services and improve service
efficiency. Therefore, the construction of a multi-layer rural financial
institution system is a long-term systematic large project. Only with
joint efforts of all parties will the system construction be promoted
and will the function of the system in driving the development of
agricultural economy be leveraged. Actual situation indicates that the
construction of the system is defective in following aspects:

\(I\) Agricultural insurance institution is not developed sufficiently

Experience of developed countries represented by the USA shows that
insurance industry has closely combined with the development of
agriculture. The development of insurance products and services
targeting agriculture are relatively mature. However, insurance
companies targeting agricultural development have not yet developed in
China in current stage. In this aspect, there is still a large gap
between China and those developed countries. Currently, China has only
published policy-based rules on the support from insurance companies for
agricultural development. More importantly, the insurance-related
supporting fund is provided for rural residents by non-agricultural
insurance institutions as agencies. Objectively speaking, natural
conditions, climates and other uncertain factors have great impact on
agricultural production. Agricultural production and operation are
exposed to relatively high risk, which is consistent with the
positioning of insurance. Therefore, it is suitable to development
agricultural insurance. In the past few years, there has been saying
that agricultural insurance companies will be established in China.
However, no substantial progress has been made in this aspect.
Meanwhile, privately-owned cooperating insurance institutions are not
developed maturely and only have limited function. These institutions
are unable to play the role of shifting risks confronted by
"agriculture, rural area and farm".

\(II\) Function of trust investment companies targeting rural areas are
not fully leveraged

Nature of agricultural economy decides the relatively low input-output
rate of the industry. The development of trust investment institutions
is much more backward than the development of relatively lagging
agricultural insurance. In current stage, few trust investment
institutions can be found in the development of rural economy, which
greatly restricts the business expansion by trust investment companies
in agricultural sector and makes it hard for trust investment companies
to leverage their functions in the process of supporting the development
of "agriculture, rural area and farmer".

\(III\) Non-governmental mutual financing cooperative grows slowly

Theoretically, the establishment of non-governmental mutual financing
cooperative will make up the deficiencies of rural financial services to
a certain extent. However, the actual situation is that function of
non-governmental mutual financing cooperatives is not leveraged in the
development process of rural finance in China. There is a large gap
between theory and practice. Generally, if a rural resident or an
agricultural enterprise has demand for loan because of fund problems,
they will choose to apply for loan from financial institutions in the
first place. However, according to internal system, financial
institutions are required to exam the loan application received, credit
status of the loan applicant, existing loan record, collateral and so
on. Moreover, it is a long time to handle the loan application and
relevant formalities are also complicated, which is obviously unable to
meet the urgent demand of the rural residents or agricultural
enterprises for fund. Non-governmental mutual financing cooperatives are
advantageous over other financial institutions with respect to loan
application, qualification review and formality-handling procedure and
able to meet the demand of rural residents or agricultural enterprises
for fund in short time. Therefore, Non-governmental mutual financing
cooperatives are "supplementary" to the function of financial
institutions. With respect to the slow development of non-governmental
mutual financing cooperatives, the country needs to push forward the
development of such organizations by policy-based guidance and support.
In summary, it is a heavy and time-consuming task to construct a
multi-layer financial institution system. All subjects of the system
need to participate into the construction. At present, there is still a
great gap between current development status and ideal status.

### 5.3 Analysis of Causes for Slow Development of Rural Finance in China

#### 5.3.1 Infrastructure

If we strictly categorize rural financial institutions and agricultural
financial institutions, regular institutions providing rural financial
services include Agricultural Bank, Postal Savings Bank, Rural
Commercial Banks, and others. Typically, they establish business
branches in township areas to directly purchase or lease the necessary
infrastructure to create financial service platforms. In this context,
the ability to successfully establish these platforms, as well as the
specific geographic location of these platforms, significantly impacts
the degree to which financial institutions can fulfill their functions.
It also has a substantial impact on the implementation of national
financial policies.

Furthermore, from the earlier discussion regarding the proportion of
business branches of agricultural financial institutions, it\'s evident
that there are still many underdeveloped areas in rural China. If the
infrastructure for rural financial development is lacking, it can
significantly hinder the effectiveness of rural financial services.
Firstly, underdeveloped infrastructure for rural financial development
can impede the expansion of financial services in rural areas.
Typically, rural financial institutions establish branches and service
platforms in county or township areas, which increases the distance
between rural households and agricultural businesses, making it more
difficult for them to access financial products and services. Even
today, many rural households still consider the functionality of banks
to be limited to savings and currency exchange, which is not conducive
to fully utilizing the functions of rural financial institutions.
Secondly, underdeveloped infrastructure in rural financial development
can lead to information asymmetry. According to standard procedures,
rural financial institutions assign professional credit officers to
collect information from rural households after receiving loan
applications. However, due to operational constraints, the number of
information collectors is relatively limited, and the distance between
financial institution branches and rural households is typically quite
long. This significantly affects the efficiency and quality of the work
of credit officers, leading to information asymmetry, which, in turn,
negatively impacts business operations. In this scenario, it can
unintentionally prolong the loan processing period and result in delayed
loans to rural households and agricultural businesses with funding
needs. Ultimately, this reduces the attractiveness of rural financial
institutions to rural households and agricultural businesses.
Additionally, the existence of this \"last mile\" challenge poses a risk
of customer attrition for rural financial institutions.

#### 5.3.2 Market

Actual situation indicates that agriculture is less competitive that
industry and service industry with respect to the access to financial
services. From the perspective of market supply, many rural financial
institutions are aware of that the enterprise profit created by
agriculture-related financial products and services is not ideal and thy
may be even faced with the risk of loss. Consequently, if rural
financial institutions have better choices, they will put rural
residents and agricultural enterprises behind when they ranking their
choices. Some rural financial institutions may even absorption depot in
rural areas and loan to non-agricultural sectors or non-rural areas,
which leads to the flowing out of capital which is supposed to use for
agricultural development, thus causing unbalance in total amounts. From
the perspective of market demand, rural residents and agricultural
enterprises are relatively difficult in accessing financial services as
stated above.

With reference to the theory of market supply and demand, if the supply
is reduced, it will be more difficult to meet the demand or the cost for
meeting the demand will be increased greatly. As for the development of
rural finance, reduction of supply makes it harder to meet the demand of
rural residents and agricultural enterprises for fund and then push
rural residents and agricultural enterprises to skip over regular
financial institutions and borrow money from relatives and friends to
solve the fund problem. In this aspect, the situation will further
reduce the reliance of the development of "agriculture, rural area and
farmer" on regular financial institutions. If the demand is reduced, the
supply will be reduced correspondingly. In the process of development,
if rural financial institutions find that less fund is needed for the
development of "agriculture, rural area and farmer", the institutions
will reduce the supply correspondingly, which will make it harder for
rural residents and agricultural enterprises to access financial
products and services. The foresaid situation will form a vicious cycle,
and each process will be repeated unceasingly. In the end, benefit of
both the supply side and demand side will be affected.

#### 5.3.3 Policies

In current stage, market failure in the process of development of rural
finance in China is relatively serious due to impact of many factors.
Therefore, function of "the visible hand" should be leveraged in this
circumstance, and policy-based tools and measures should be used to
solve problems caused by market failure. For example, the country will
revise laws and regulations to guarantee that a favorable environment is
provided for the development rural finance and request some rural
financial institutions to set up service outlets or service platforms in
grass-root units to promote the development of rural finance. However,
the government, being "the visible hand", is not always powerful.
Failures may be caused by some internal and external factors. Such
failures are referred to as the government failures.

\(I\) "Government failure" in rural finance construction

\(1\) Usually, outlets and financial service platforms of rural
financial institutions are set up in county towns or towns. Generally
speaking, there are much more financial institution outlets and service
platforms in county towns than in towns. There are many reasons for the
phenomena. The most important factor is probably that staffs of these
outlets and platforms live in county towns and towns. If the outlets and
platforms are established in rural areas, it will be inconvenient for
these staffs. The distribution of outlets and platforms leads to long
distance from rural financial infrastructure to rural areas, thus
causing negative problems like information asymmetry, which is not
favorable for healthy development of rural finance.

\(2\) To push forward the development of rural finance, the country
designates a number of institutions mainly engaged in rural financial
business, such as Rural Credit Cooperatives, intending to pushing
forward the development of rural financial business with these financial
institution outlets as carriers. However, many rural financial
institutions or agriculture-related financial institutions focus on
business development in urban areas, only carry out business targeting
large-scale agricultural enterprises and rich rural residents and keep
distance with impoverished rural residents and small and medium-sized
enterprises due to the existence of market failure. Obviously, these
institutions run in the opposite direction against the intention of the
country. 

\(II\) "Government failure" in rural finance regulation

Currently, the regulation of rural finance is still a heavy burden and
takes a long time. There are still problems in the regulation work.
Firstly, rise in income level of rural areas in current stage creates
increasing demand of rural areas for financial management. Flow
direction of the large amount of surplus fund has relatively important
impact. Currently, regulation on the surplus fund in China is not
effective enough, which causes vile events, such as illegal
fund-raising, and creates adverse impact on social development. In
addition, some petty loan companies are developed in the market to
respond to the imbalance between financial supply and financial demand
in rural areas. These petty loan companies are small in scale but seeded
and growing in the soil of rural areas. Therefore, they are able to meet
financing demand of rural areas. In the process of development, the
country also publishes preferential policies to support the development
of these petty loan companies. However, as the policies are not open
enough in the implementation process and institutions and talents with
the power of regulation are all seated in county town, some mass events
occur due to ineffective regulation. Generally speaking, government
failure is mainly reflected in two aspects: excessive regulation and
inadequate regulation.

\(1\) Excessive regulation

As the supply of and demand for financial products and services in rural
areas are unbalanced for long term and the supply is less than demand,
many petty loan institutions are developed gradually and increase
financial supply in rural areas, thus playing an important role in
meeting credit demand of rural residents. However, petty loan
institutions are often characterized in the small development scale and
low system construction and risk management level. moreover, excessive
commercialization of these institutions will result in crisis. To cope
with this situation, the country raises up the market admission
threshold for these institutions: As the market position of these petty
loan institutions is not specified in laws issued before, these petty
loan institutions are not eligible for enjoying preferential policies
about the development of rural finance which are issued by the country.
The People's Bank of China specifies the business scope of these
institutions. For example, these institutions are not allowed to absorb
deposit of the public in order to improve the safety level of enterprise
operation and lower the probability of risk factors. Some national and
regional preferential policies only target financial institutions like
the Rural Credit Cooperatives. The petty loan institutions are excluded
from the policies, which invisibly leads to unfairness in market
competition and tends to cause "monopoly" in some sectors. The situation
is harmful to the healthy development of rural finance.

\(2\) Inadequate regulation

According to laws of China, the CBRC has the authority of regulating
financial institutions. In practice, local offices of CBRC focus on the
regulation of large-scale and medium-sized banks and delegate power of
regulation on rural petty loan institutions to grass-root offices. Due
to the busy work on regulation, inadequate number of working personnel
and lack of professional talents, the regulation on rural petty loan
institutions is ineffective or even not conducted, which causes some
serious events. Problems caused by ineffective regulation will cause
loss of attractiveness of rural petty loan institutions and also harm
the healthy development of rural finance.

### 5.4 Summary

The analysis of reasons for current situation of under-developed rural
finance shows that fundamental factors restricting the development of
rural finance includes four aspects: Firstly, "small, scattered and
remote" natural villages lead to difficulty in rational and concentrated
construction of financial infrastructure, thus leading to "poor contact"
between the supply side and demand side and causing information
asymmetry. Secondly, high service cost leads to difficulty in
sustainable supply of rural financial services. Thirdly, long-term
inadequate supply in the market creates a vicious cycle which leads to
market depression. Fourthly, inadequate power of regulation institutions
causes difficulty in regulation.

The dependent variable in this study is the financial development level

at a certain cost. Previous literature often uses actual financial
indicators obtained in a specific region as measures, such as the
presence or absence of bank loans and the total amount of loans received
(He Ren et al., 2012; Lu Yajuan, 2014). Some scholars have used the sum

funds over the years to measure the financial development level of
households at the provincial level (Gao Yuandong and Zhang Weiguo, 2014;
Hu Zongyi and Luo Liudan, 2016). Drawing from previous research methods,
this study employs the per capita amount of rural loans in various
provinces to measure the level of financial development in rural areas.

The independent variable is the extent of Internet penetration in rural
areas. Given considerations from previous literature and data
availability, the study selects the macro-level measure of internet
coverage (referred to as \"internet user coverage\") among the rural
population in each province. Internet user coverage is typically
measured as the proportion of rural internet users to the total rural
population. A higher proportion indicates a broader internet coverage
among rural populations. As there is no available data on rural internet
users in national statistics over the years, this study borrows the
method commonly used in previous research to estimate the number of
rural internet users, which involves calculating the product of the
total number of internet users in each province and the proportion of
urban and rural internet users.

In terms of control variables, referencing literature on empirical
research in rural finance (Gao Yuandong, Wen Tao, Wang Xiaohua, 2013; Hu
Yizong and Li Peng, 2013), regions with higher per capita income in
rural areas tend to have a higher level of financial services. In terms
of financial situations, the financial status of regional governments is
a critical factor that may influence the coverage of local credit
services. Recognizing the importance of financial expenditure related to
agriculture in rural finance, areas with higher financial support for
agriculture tend to have more significant investments in rural
infrastructure and welfare development. At the same time, the growth
rate and structure of the social productivity value within rural areas
may also affect the level of financial services in the locality, such as
the speed and intensity of credit extension. Based on these
considerations, this study includes the following control variables: per
capita annual income of rural households, per capita financial

# Chapter 7 The Current Status of the Application of Digital Technology in Rural Financial Development

Based on the empirical research findings mentioned earlier, this chapter
will further analyze how digital technology promotes rural finance
development at the practical application level. It will specifically
discuss the advantages of these applications, the application models,
and the current issues in the application phase.

According to Xie Ping, Zou Chuanwei, and Liu Haier (2014), digital
finance can be categorized into six forms, including financial
digitization (such as online banks, mobile banking, online securities
companies, online insurance companies, online financial trading
platforms, and online sales of financial products), mobile payments and
third-party payments, digital currencies, credit scoring and online
loans based on big data, P2P online loans, and crowdfunding. Each of
these different forms of digital finance has unique characteristics and
can contribute to the inclusive development of rural finance at various
levels.

**7.1 The Advantages of the Application of Digital Technology in Rural
Financial Development**

The development of rural finance needs to overcome the financial
exclusion caused by accessibility barriers. Digital finance leverages
its advantages primarily in four ways to overcome traditional barriers
in rural financial services:

1.  Digital platforms help improve rural financial infrastructure.

2.  Digital finance enhances the accessibility of financial services for
    rural households.

3.  Digital finance is a significant factor associated with reducing the
    costs associated with rural financial services.

4.  Digital technology aids in enhancing the regulatory capacity of
    rural finance.

**7.1.1 Enhancing Rural Financial Infrastructure**

The characteristics of \"small, scattered, and remote\" natural villages
in rural areas have resulted in a fundamental lack of information, which
is one of the root causes of the underdevelopment of rural finance. To
address this problem, there are primarily two approaches: the
establishment of physical infrastructure and the introduction of mobile
digital platforms. This study believes that the development of digital
finance can alleviate the information scarcity caused by geographical
challenges, improving rural financial infrastructure at a lower cost.
The following sections will use the rapidly expanding mobile digital
platform and the ongoing development of the rural household credit
network as examples to illustrate the advantages of digital technology
in promoting rural financial development.

I. Promotion of Mobile Digital Platforms:

1\. Promotion of Information Exchange through Mature Technology: One of
the fundamental issues causing the underdevelopment of rural finance is
the lack of information due to the characteristics of natural villages
in rural areas, which are often small, scattered, and remote. This leads
to difficulties in establishing rural financial infrastructure,
resulting in inadequate information flow between both the supply and
demand sides of financial services. To address the information scarcity
caused by these geographical challenges, it is essential to find a means
of information exchange that is not restricted by geographical location.
Mobile digital technology, which is not constrained by geography,
facilitates communication and information exchange between points and
multiple locations at any distance. Moreover, rural wireless
communication networks have achieved nearly full coverage, and mobile
payment technology is relatively mature. These factors can significantly
enhance information exchange in rural financial services. Given the
rapid development of information technology, a global information
revolution is underway. By promoting the use of mobile digital platforms
in financial services, we can enable rural residents to be a part of
this information revolution, gradually digitizing their lives in rural
areas and enriching financial services related to agriculture.

2.  Reduction of Costs in Non-Physical Platforms: The cost and
    convenience of providing and accessing financial services through
    mobile digital platforms are significantly lower than traditional
    physical platforms. In terms of service provision, there is no need
    to establish physical financial branches. Instead, services are
    offered through self-service platforms, saving expenses related to
    leasing physical spaces, equipment wear and tear, and staff
    salaries. The cost of mobile digital platforms is approximately 85%
    lower than that of traditional counter transactions. Additionally,
    rural residents can access convenient financial services from the
    comfort of their homes, eliminating the need for complicated
    transportation and long queues. Furthermore, digital financial
    services not only include the \"big three\" rural financial services
    like savings, transfers, and remittances but also offer a diverse
    range of services such as online shopping, wealth management, mutual
    fund investments, crowdfunding, etc. This diversification reduces
    transaction costs, enhances information transparency and symmetry,
    and allows rural residents to access a broader range of financial
    services at lower costs.

3.  Rapid Promotion Leading to the Transformation of Rural Financial
    Service Models: China\'s rural areas are already well-equipped to
    promote mobile digital financial platforms. In terms of mobile
    digital financial platform clients, rural areas have high and
    rapidly growing rates of digital penetration. The number of rural
    mobile internet users in China is increasing annually, and the
    conditions for expanding mobile digital platforms to clients are
    already in place. On the supply side, traditional financial
    institutions are actively transitioning to digital networks. Large
    state-owned banks such as Industrial and Commercial Bank and China
    Merchants Bank have already established digital customer service
    platforms. Furthermore, with the rapid proliferation of smartphones
    and digital technology in rural areas, the rural financial services
    market is expanding. Agricultural financial products are becoming
    more innovative in terms of targeting, breadth, and depth of
    services, prompting traditional financial institutions like rural
    credit cooperatives to transition. The rural financial industry is
    entering a period of rapid development, driving the transformation
    of rural financial service models, which, in turn, provides strong
    support for rural economic development and national urbanization
    efforts.

In summary, mobile digital platforms serve as the foundation of rural
finance with mature technology, lower costs, and ease of promotion.
Although the development of rural financial infrastructure in China has
received significant support from the government, there are still many
deficiencies. Mobile digital financial platforms are needed as a
complementary solution. Furthermore, to promote inclusive rural economic
development and ensure the synchronous development of rural and urban
finance, the establishment of mobile digital financial platforms is
essential.

II\. Establishment of Rural Household Credit Networks:

In China, the traditional credit reporting system is primarily led by
the People\'s Bank of China. Currently, there are credit reporting
systems for both enterprises and individuals. However, these systems
only cover a limited group of people who have had credit relationships
with banks. The coverage is narrow, and the credit records are
incomplete. Notably, 23% of the data in these systems come from urban
residents, leaving a significant portion of the rural population
unaccounted for. As of the end of 2015, only around 160 million rural
households had established credit profiles, with more than a billion
creditworthy rural residents remaining without recognized credit
records. This inadequacy of the People\'s Bank of China\'s credit
reporting system is closely related to the delayed development of rural
credit environments and the frequent occurrence of \"difficulty in
obtaining loans\" in rural areas.

Insufficient information is a key factor contributing to difficulties in
financing for rural households and making it costlier. Financial
institutions are either unable to access the credit information of such
customers or find the cost of obtaining it prohibitively high, leading
to reluctance in extending loans. The fundamental problem in developing
rural finance is the issue of information. The critical solution to this
problem, facilitated by digital technology, lies in the application of
big data. The establishment of a rural credit reporting network can help
drive the improvement of rural financial infrastructure.

Specifically, the development of digital technology can assist in
consolidating scattered information about rural households, reducing the
cost of information retrieval. Big data credit reporting incorporates a
portion of the population not covered by the People\'s Bank of China\'s
credit system, significantly addressing the shortcomings of traditional
credit reporting in terms of coverage. Rural digital financial
institutions use big data technology to gather information about rural
residents in the digital realm, record their behavioral patterns, and
conduct comprehensive analyses to more accurately assess their repayment
capacity and willingness. This approach rectifies the severe
insufficiency in the rural credit reporting system. Presently, China\'s
big data credit reporting systems primarily fall into three categories:

1.  E-commerce Platforms: These platforms act as third-party
    organizations that provide their credit data to other companies for
    a fee, engaging in commercial credit reporting.

2.  P2P (Peer-to-Peer) Platforms\' Proprietary Credit Systems: Various
    P2P platforms create their own credit systems, mainly using digital
    technology and their designed assessment models to offer reference
    points for their credit operations.

3.  Third-Party Credit Reporting Platforms for the Same Industry: This
    type of platform complements the People\'s Bank of China\'s credit
    system. For instance, Shanghai Zhima Credit Information Co., Ltd.,
    under the People\'s Bank of China, has developed the Network
    Financial Credit Sharing System (NFCS). Such platforms are designed
    to establish information-sharing systems. These systems collect
    customer information from both sides of P2P lending platforms, then
    integrate and process the data to provide standardized credit
    products for participating organizations. These platforms contribute
    to mitigating information asymmetry between lenders and borrowers,
    thereby averting credit risk.

In contrast to traditional credit data sources, big data credit
reporting is more extensive, with lower costs for acquiring and
processing data. This significantly reduces loan risk and costs,
enabling more rural customers to access loan funds, ultimately promoting
inclusive rural finance. Commercial banks already possess vast amounts
of data, but this data mainly pertains to structured information, such
as customers\' accounts, fund transfers, assets, liabilities, etc. This
data only allows for the assessment of customers\' repayment capacity
but does not gauge their willingness to repay. Rural financial
institutions can tap into unstructured information about rural
customers, with rural credit cooperatives, rural commercial banks,
customer managers, and agents entering this information into digital
systems. This unstructured information encompasses aspects that are
difficult to put into words, such as who may be more likely to default
or engage in dishonest financial practices. Modern digital technology is
essential for extracting information from non-structured data sources,
including call records, social network chat information, audio
recordings of customer manager visits, customer service audio, branch
video, online banking records, and e-commerce transaction traces.

These initiatives can significantly contribute to enhancing rural credit
information and, in turn, help bridge the information gap in rural
finance, ultimately facilitating the development of rural finance in
China.

Building a big data credit reporting network in rural areas, based on
the existing information infrastructure, can involve expanding
information collection in four key areas:

1.  Integration with Social Networking Platforms and E-commerce
    Platforms: This allows for the collection of customer behavioral
    information. Behavioral data can provide insights into a customer\'s
    willingness to repay, which traditional financial institutions\'
    credit reporting can\'t assess, as it typically focuses on repayment
    capacity.

2.  Integration of Information from Various Government Departments: This
    includes data retained by government departments such as Industry
    and Commerce, Taxation, the Court, the Procuratorate, Public
    Security, Political and Legal Affairs Committees, and Family
    Planning Commissions.

3.  Integration of Records from Utility Providers: This involves
    integrating data related to water, electricity, gas,
    telecommunications operators, and hospitals, among others.

4.  Leveraging the Role of Agents in Rural Areas: Make use of various
    institutions, such as China Mobile, China Telecom, China Unicom, and
    village clinics, as agents. Encourage them to input rural
    households\' information into the system.

Additionally, the process through which rural households obtain basic
financial services and financing services is also a means for them to
learn about financial knowledge and enhance their creditworthiness.
Digital development can contribute to improving the financial education
level of rural households and fostering credit awareness. On one hand,
channels like mobile banking, online banking, and mobile apps can be
used to disseminate relevant financial knowledge. On the other hand,
social networks can play a significant role. For instance, banks can
assess a borrower\'s creditworthiness by referencing their social
network information. Furthermore, many young farmers use communication
apps like QQ, WeChat, and Weibo to exchange information. Regulatory
authorities can use these social networks to share basic financial
knowledge. This helps rural households gain a better understanding of
financial concepts.

**7.1.2 Lowering the Cost of Rural Financial Services**

The primary reason for the weak capacity of rural economic and financial
services is the high cost, making it difficult for financial
institutions on the supply side to reach the \"threshold\" of providing
services with sufficient profits. As a result, the majority of rural
households are excluded from financial services, missing out on
financial inclusion. Specifically, digital finance can reduce the cost
of rural financial services from two dimensions.

(1) Aggregating Multiple Financial Services into One:

Rural financial service demands are characterized by a large volume of
low-cost individual transactions. This characteristic leads to high
supply costs in rural financial services. Economically, this type of
market demand is defined as a \"long-tail market\" or a \"niche
market,\" where the cost can be minimized by using a large sales base to
provide affordable products or services. This approach is impossible for
traditional manual operations but is feasible under digital technology
conditions. The practice of digital finance in rural areas has proven
its feasibility.

At the technical level, digital finance operates through a network of
physical components such as computers, network cables, switches, and
routers. The most basic unit of computation is the computer, which is
equipped with the power to process information efficiently. In the
information revolution, the speed of processing various information by
computer programs far exceeds human capabilities. Another advantage is
its strong aggregation ability. Different types of financial service
products cannot be combined for humans. However, for a computer, these
are different software execution processes, ultimately processing in
binary, and they can be combined easily. Thus, computers have
significantly faster processing speeds and stronger aggregation
capabilities, the more data they need to handle. For financial services,
a single computer system\'s one-second processing capacity can be
equivalent to a person\'s day\'s worth of work or even more. As long as
the program is strictly written according to a fixed process, with
timely and reliable information collection, the computer information
system can effectively and quickly complete the work. With China\'s
rural \"Triple Network Convergence\" project, we can be confident that
digital finance terminals will achieve full coverage in rural areas.
When various mobile customer terminals in rural digital finance submit
service request information for various types of loan services, such as
weddings, funerals, and others, to the digital financial service server
computer in the specified format, the computer works according to the
established software system\'s process. It can simultaneously serve many
or even a vast number of customers, and, for the computer, the
transaction cost of these many customers applying for service is almost
the same as a single customer.

Additionally, digital technology exhibits a strong aggregation effect,
powerful enough to allow people all over the world to see the same
information or send information to the same place. Given the vast rural
population in China, suppose tens of thousands of people apply for
financial services through digital technology simultaneously, each
person applying for one thousand yuan. In that case, for the computer,
this transaction is one billion yuan, and the service cost is nearly
identical to an individual applying for one billion yuan.

(2) Reducing the Cost of Single Rural Financial Services:

Digital technology can effectively address the challenge of high
transaction costs in rural financial services, primarily by reducing the
costs caused by \"distance\" through mobile digital payments. Digital
information integration and analysis technology lowers the costs caused
by difficulties in information retrieval. Digital resource allocation
benefits the profitability prospects of rural-related industries,
reducing the costs of risk assessment and collateral risk management.
Digital technology plays a significant role in social resource
allocation, acting as a bridge between the market and planning. It
introduces planning into the market, and planning fulfills the market.
Rural products and collateral assets can go online, enabling them to
directly connect with the market, enhancing the profitability prospects
of rural-related industries, and reducing the costs of risk assessment
and collateral risk management. Furthermore, transactions conducted on
online platforms leave behind transaction information, which can serve
as the primary source of customer assessment for financial service
providers. In a well-informed environment, loans and financial services
may not even require collateral.

In conclusion, digital technology can significantly reduce the cost of
rural financial services, thus improving the competitiveness of rural
customers in obtaining financial services and allowing financial service
providers to achieve sustainable development.

**7.1.3 Enhancing the Accessibility of Rural Financial Markets for
Financing**

Rural financial markets suffer from limited service providers, leading
to a lack of competitive dynamics and restricting the development of the
financing side in rural finance. Currently, large banks possess
substantial strength, making it challenging for smaller financial
institutions to challenge them. However, digital finance has the
capability to capture market share, urging traditional financial
institutions to actively participate in competition in the rural
financial market, thus increasing the availability of financing. This
leads to a more dynamic market and an elevation in the quality of
financial products and services through innovation.

In comparison to traditional rural financial institutions, digital
finance boasts three key advantages:

First, it aligns closely with the digital economy and offers \'small yet
beautiful\' products. The digital economy encompasses a series of
commercial activities conducted in the digital sphere. Currently, the
digital economy is experiencing rapid growth, and e-commerce is
significantly impacting traditional markets and trade fairs. As the
financial industry is intertwined with and services the economy, the
development of the digital economy promotes the growth of digital
finance. Digital financial service providers can easily analyze a
customer\'s profit potential and market opportunities through digital
transaction records and other information. Additionally, obtaining
financial services such as loans is made easy, quick, and convenient
compared to the complex processes of traditional financial services.

Second, it utilizes digital technology as a production tool, which
results in high production efficiency and rapid dissemination. As
mentioned earlier, digital technology\'s powerful aggregation and batch
processing capabilities make digital finance highly efficient in its
operations. It has low production costs, extensive market coverage, and
leverages \'word of mouth\' to transmit information, leading to an
explosive impact. Therefore, it can efficiently produce and quickly
capture market share.

Third, it leverages the advantages of digital resource allocation.
Digital finance can reconfigure resources through the vast digital
marketplace, using products produced in rural industries with lower
sales volumes or assets like land and property as collateral. This
process enhances the efficiency of resource recycling, encourages the
development of rural-related industries, and significantly reduces the
non-performing asset ratio compared to traditional rural financial
services.

**7.1.4 Enhance Rural Financial Regulatory Capacity**

Economic activities must be conducted under regulation, and unregulated
markets, especially financial markets, are prone to social issues.
Robust regulation is key to addressing the challenges in rural financial
policies, and enhancing China\'s rural financial regulatory capacity is
crucial. It\'s feasible to enhance rural financial regulatory capacity
through digital means for the following reasons:

1.Regulation is based on information, and digital technology excels in
information collection and processing, enabling remote monitoring by
professionals. Government agencies rely on information for financial
regulation. Digital tools can efficiently collect crucial information
and, through batch processing by professionals and information systems,
enable remote monitoring of financial institutions at the grassroots
level. Additionally, collaboration with local regulatory authorities
allows for timely information verification, enhancing the regulatory
capabilities of local offices.

2.Digital applications promote transparency, enabling social oversight.
Digital applications have strong transparency characteristics.
Transactions by some new financial companies can be displayed online. If
customers notice issues with a particular internet financial company\'s
operations, they can publish this information online. Regulatory
authorities can then investigate with specialized tools to determine if
there is a genuine problem. If issues exist, the authority can order
corrective actions. If not, the misinformation is dispelled. Public
engagement has the potential to produce significant regulatory effects.

Mobile digital technologies, in particular, can play a vital role in
rural financial regulation. With mobile phones moving toward real-name
registration, phone numbers can serve as identity credentials. Users can
store value on their mobile phones, effectively creating virtual
accounts tied to their phone numbers. These numbers function similarly
to deposit accounts and can be accessed from anywhere at any time. With
access to call logs, contacts, and text messages, mobile service
providers have insight into users\' core social networks. This
information can be used to evaluate borrowers\' creditworthiness and
facilitate the monitoring of credit activities. Moreover, mobile
operators can use their networks for \"social punishment\" (e.g.,
suspending communication services) to control default and moral hazards
among borrowers and their agents (e.g., institutions), effectively
supervising both individual borrowers and institutional operations.

In summary, a combination of remote and local supervision, automated and
manual regulation, professional and public oversight can bolster
regulatory capabilities. This approach allows rural digital finance to
develop under effective regulatory oversight.

**7.2.3 Traditional Financial Institutions are Creating a New Path for
\"Digital + Rural Finance\" Development**

In the context of lending between China\'s rural areas and rural
financial institutions, traditional rural financial institutions face
challenges such as difficulty in expanding their scale and securing
financing channels. The current rural credit cooperatives often lack
support from commercial economies, and although there is direct
cooperation between rural economic development and rural financial
institutions, there is a lack of a solid foundation, leading to low
mutual trust, low repayment capacity, and long repayment periods. This
situation prevents rural financial institutions from bridging the credit
gap between small farmers and large banks, resulting in difficulties in
bank loans and rural loans.

In the digital era, communication speed for economic development has
increased, and the distance between different segments has been reduced.
State-owned commercial banks have gradually shifted their focus towards
supporting urban economic development. As a result, they have started to
withdraw from rural financial markets. Local banks have been pushed
aside by traditional state-owned banks, and traditional credit concepts
have affected the expected role of \"Digital +.\" This has created
competition between small enterprises, rural financial institutions,
local credit cooperatives, and agricultural banks for high-quality
customer resources.

On the other hand, in the rural financial sector, the limitations of
traditional financial models in the physical space have been broken by
the continuous entry and deepening of digital finance. With more and
more farmers using mobile devices such as smartphones, the cost of
financial services has decreased. Farmers can access various financial
services online anytime, anywhere, breaking the monopoly of rural
financial institutions. To achieve the transformation and development of
rural financial institutions, traditional financial institutions must
integrate digital financial business with their own circumstances and
develop a sense of crisis.

At present, in China, traditional financial institutions in inclusive
rural financial institutions mainly include policy banks that provide
substantial and accessible financial services to \"agriculture, rural
areas, and farmers,\" especially low-income rural households. This
includes the Agricultural Development Bank of China, the Agricultural
Bank of China, the China Postal Savings Bank, rural cooperative
financial institutions, and new rural financial institutions. The number
of major rural financial institutions changed from 2012 to 2016, as
shown in Table 7.3. It indicates that during this period, the total
number of major rural financial institutions had a slight upward trend.
By 2016, there were a total of 3,785 rural financial institutions
approved to operate nationwide, including 2,281 rural cooperative
financial institutions (rural commercial banks, rural cooperative banks,
rural credit cooperatives), and 1,506 institutions from China Postal
Savings Bank and new rural financial institutions (village and township
banks, loan companies, rural mutual assistance funds).

the \"Youzhanggui\" (Postal Counter) e-commerce platform, targeting
rural e-commerce. For \"Youzhanggui\" customers in rural areas, the bank
introduced digital lending products such as \"Counter Loan,\" achieving
end-to-end online loan application, credit approval, and simplifying the
financial service process. This initiative has resulted in a unique
\"digital finance + rural e-commerce\" network-based lending model that
significantly enhances support for financing services in rural areas.

China Postal Savings Bank has made substantial efforts in product
innovation and the construction of financial networks and platforms in
rural areas. From 2013 to 2015, the bank achieved an average annual
compound growth rate of 38% in rural loans. It has cumulatively issued
over 1.1 trillion yuan in small personal loans, with an average loan
amount of just 7,000 yuan, effectively addressing the shortage of
operational funds for nearly 9.2 million rural households.

(3) Rural Credit Cooperatives and Other Rural Cooperative Financial
    Institutions

The Rural Credit Union Payment Clearing System is a payment and clearing
platform developed using modern computer networks and digital technology
based on the payment and settlement needs of national rural credit
cooperatives, rural cooperative banks, and rural commercial banks. This
system combines funds clearing and information services into one
platform and enables real-time electronic remittances, rural credit
union bank drafts, and personal account deposits and withdrawals for
urban and rural customers, particularly in rural areas.

In the context of the \"digital+\" strategy, the Rural Credit Union Fund
Clearing Center has leveraged digital technologies such as big data and
cloud computing to innovate and develop payment services. Building on
the Rural Credit Union Payment Clearing Platform, they have introduced a
range of shared innovative products and services, including \"Life
e-Butler,\" \"WeChat e-Services,\" \"Rural Credit e-Payment,\" and
\"LiNong Mall.\" These offerings allow rural customers to enjoy the
convenience of digital financial services without leaving their homes,
effectively meeting diverse financial and payment needs in rural areas.

Currently, the system covers nearly 80,000 branches and outlets of rural
credit cooperatives, rural cooperative banks, rural commercial banks,
and township banks in 30 provinces and regions across mainland China.
This extension of payment and settlement services to remote rural areas
has become an important component of China\'s payment system, improving
the previously inadequate, limited, and single-function rural payment
and settlement channels.

In addition, various provincial rural credit cooperatives and other
rural cooperative financial institutions have introduced various digital
financial products. Taking Anhui Rural Credit Cooperatives as an
example, Anhui Rural Credit Cooperatives Union has partnered with 83
rural commercial banks in Anhui Province to jointly launch an innovative
financial product called the \"Community e-Bank.\" This product is a
comprehensive mobile financial services platform that combines quick
payments, convenience, and credit value enhancement. People can use a
mobile app to perform basic financial services such as account
inquiries, transfers, mobile phone top-ups, flight reservations, and
utility bill payments. It also offers settlement services through QR
code payments and mobile number payments. As of the end of 2015, Anhui
Rural Credit Cooperatives\' \"Community e-Bank\" had accumulated 28,600
online merchants, 1.2734 million transactions, and a transaction volume
of 82.85 million yuan.

**7.3 The Challenges in the Application of Digital Finance in Rural
Financial Development**

In recent years, the development of digital finance has gained
significant momentum, reaching historical highs in terms of the number
of enterprises and overall scale. During this rapid growth, some digital
financial platforms have ventured into rural markets. Companies like
Alibaba and JD.com, for example, are trying to promote the use of
digital finance in rural financial development through e-commerce and
P2P lending models. While they have achieved some success, there are
still challenges in terms of practical application.

**7.3.1 Rural Digital Infrastructure Remains Weak**

Although China has made significant progress in its digital
infrastructure, many rural areas still lack adequate coverage. Compared
to urban areas, rural regions often have weaker economic foundations and
more dispersed populations, resulting in higher costs for broadband
construction and maintenance. Consequently, broadband coverage in rural
areas is significantly lower than in urban areas. In 2018, the Chinese
government allocated over 140 billion RMB to launch the \"Broadband into
Villages\" and \"Information Benefits Farmers\" projects, aiming to
enhance broadband service in rural and remote areas. The plan includes
connecting broadband to 50,000 administrative villages without previous
access and upgrading broadband for over 30 million rural households.
Additionally, it involves providing free broadband installation for some
extremely impoverished households and households registered as part of
the national poverty alleviation program.

A survey conducted by the China Agricultural University\'s School of
Economics and Management in July 2017 sheds light on the current state
of China\'s rural digital infrastructure. The survey, conducted using a
stratified random sampling method, included three counties in Henan
Province. In each county, three townships were selected based on their
economic development levels, and two natural villages were randomly
chosen in each township. The sample size consisted of 214 valid
responses. The findings revealed that only 47.6% of rural households
owned smartphones capable of accessing the internet, and 24.3% had
computers with internet access. Additionally, only one household
reported having used digital lending or crowdfunding services, while
nearly 78.9% of respondents who hadn\'t used these services cited their
reasons as \"no exposure to or lack of knowledge about them.\" These
survey results highlight the impact of rural households\' inadequate
digital infrastructure on their awareness, knowledge, and means to
access financial services through digital technology, significantly
constraining the accessibility of financial services in rural areas.

**7.3.2 The Diversity of Risks in Rural Financial Digital Applications**

Digitization has promoted the development of rural inclusive finance by
providing more convenient and accessible transaction methods and
channels for rural financial transactions, thus extending financial
services to rural communities. However, the characteristic of being
unaffected by space and geographical constraints has also led to the
faster spread of financial risks in rural areas. Compared to urban
financial markets, rural financial markets are characterized by lower
regulatory standards, imperfect market environments, and weaker risk
prevention and control capabilities of market participants. These issues
can lead to higher risks in rural finance, and the integration of
digital technology with rural finance not only generates more risks but
also accelerates their transmission.

According to data released by the People\'s Bank of China, as shown in

financial risks due to various imperfect mechanisms.

1.  Market Risk: This is also known as systemic risk and refers to the
    risk of investment return variations due to global and common
    factors that are beyond control. This type of risk includes war,
    major social unrest, financial crises, unexpected significant
    natural disasters, and drastic market price fluctuations. Typically,
    the probability of events like war, significant social unrest, or
    financial crises is low. However, the likelihood of unexpected
    significant natural disasters and severe market price fluctuations
    is much higher, particularly when it comes to financing projects
    related to rural agriculture. For example, digital finance in the
    agricultural supply chain or digital finance agricultural projects
    can be significantly affected by the risk of unexpected significant
    natural disasters and severe market price fluctuations.

2.  Credit Risk: Also known as default risk, this is the risk that
    arises when financial service recipients fail to fulfill their
    obligations as agreed in contracts, resulting in economic losses. It
    primarily refers to the risk that borrowers may not be able to meet
    their debt service obligations, causing lenders to receive lower
    returns than expected. The \"digital + rural finance\" credit risk
    mainly originates from two aspects. Firstly, it comes from the
    credit risk of rural digital financial platforms. Currently, there
    is a proliferation of various digital financial platforms with
    varying degrees of reliability. According to data from
    Wangdaizhijia, as of May 2018, the number of online lending
    platforms in China reached 1,872, and the cumulative number of
    problematic platforms reached 2,058. As shown in Figure 7.10, the
    number of P2P platforms has been in a significant decline since
    2015.

Figure 7.10 Number of P2P Online Lending Platforms and Problematic
Platforms from 2014 to 2018.

Data Source: Wangdaizhijia (P2P Online Lending House)

The entire digital financial transaction taking place on virtual
platforms leads to digital companies using false credit ratings to
deceive users into investing. Additionally, the purpose of funds is not
verifiable, making it extremely easy for fraud to occur. Some platforms
even misrepresent the use of funds, create fictitious borrowers, and do
not adhere to the terms of the contracts. Without any third-party
guarantee, when issues arise, the funds become irrecoverable, resulting
in significant losses. Coupled with the limited financial knowledge of
rural residents, who often struggle to distinguish between the quality
of platforms, their investments are made blindly, leading to even
greater risks.

On the other hand, credit risk also arises from rural fund seekers.
Since the scale of online loans in rural areas is relatively small and
dispersed, rural fund seekers often cannot provide collateral.
Therefore, if problems occur, funds become non-recoverable, leading to
bad debts. This is a primary reason for credit risk among rural fund
seekers. Moreover, agricultural activities are closely tied to nature,
and they inherently possess a high degree of unpredictability. Adverse
weather conditions or poor harvests can lead to fund shortages, making
it impossible for timely repayments, which may affect the entire digital
financial platform\'s fund chain, causing uncontrollable risk. Companies
like Ant Financial Group and JD Finance collect data on customer
transaction flows and fund flows to create their own credit systems.
This enables them to offer loans to customers based on their own credit
standards, reducing credit risk.

3.  Operational risk: which arises from errors or even fraud due to
    factors such as employees, processes, infrastructure, technology, or
    other elements that impact digital financial business operations,
    resulting in financial losses. Operational risk can manifest in
    various aspects of rural digital financial operations, including
    internal fraud, external fraud, employee behavior, workplace
    security, clients, products, business practices, damage to tangible
    assets, business interruptions, system failures, transaction
    execution, delivery, and process management.

To be specific, operational risks in the context of \"digital + rural
finance\" primarily include operational risks within financial
institutions and operational risks among rural clients. The primary
cause of internal operational risk is losses incurred by financial
institution employees due to their unfamiliarity with financial
operation systems. When digital technology and rural finance are
combined to create new financial services, it is not uncommon for
employees to make errors due to their lack of familiarity with these
operations. Additionally, many rural digital financial platforms are
still in the developmental stage and prioritize product development,
platform fund accumulation, without establishing a comprehensive
internal control system, leading to a weaker capacity to withstand risks
and the spread of these risks.

In rural areas, it\'s crucial to emphasize client operational risk.
Rural populations often lack knowledge of digital financial services and
may not fully comprehend the operational procedures, leading to mistakes
and significant financial losses. Moreover, rural clients may not
adequately protect themselves from viruses on payment terminals when
making online payments, which can lead to the theft of personal
information and online banking passwords, resulting in financial losses.

4.  Liquidity risk. This risk refers to financial service providers
    having the ability to repay but being unable to obtain sufficient
    funds in a timely manner or being unable to obtain sufficient funds
    at a reasonable cost to cope with asset growth or the payment of
    maturing debts. The occurrence of credit risk and operational risk
    can lead to insufficient fund flow, making the causes of liquidity
    risk highly complex. Digital finance has an amplifying effect on
    liquidity risk, as transactions take place on virtual digital
    platforms and may escape the traditional financial liquidity
    regulatory system. It may even escape the constraints of real
    currency, thus increasing potential risks. Particularly in rural
    areas, where funds are small and dispersed, liquidity risk is more
    likely to emerge. For instance, many peer-to-peer lending platforms
    are credit intermediaries that require online funds to be connected
    with offline projects. Some P2P platforms set up to fraudulently
    absorb funds from investors for their own consumption or offline
    projects. Once information about poorly performing or unfavorable
    crowdfunding projects leaks, they are prone to runs, thereby
    creating liquidity risk. The integration of digital finance and
    rural finance accelerates the spread of liquidity risk, potentially
    affecting the entire rural financial system and leading to
    difficulties in fund turnover.

**7.3.3The Rural Big Data Credit System Still Needs Further
Improvement**

1\. Diverse Credit Reporting Methods: Each rural digital financial
institution has its own credit reporting methods. The various credit
reporting agencies lack standardized criteria in terms of data sources,
credit scoring models, and credit standards, resulting in significant
differences among them. This diversity hinders the comprehensiveness and
accuracy of credit reporting data.

2.  Limited Integration with Central Bank\'s Credit System: China\'s
    central bank\'s credit system is relatively closed and has strict
    requirements for data accuracy, standardization, security, and
    customer privacy protection. Many digital financial enterprises,
    given their rapid growth and varying quality, often cannot meet
    these requirements. As a result, it\'s challenging to integrate
    digital credit reporting data with the central bank\'s credit
    system, and information resources cannot be easily shared.

To establish a robust credit reporting system in rural areas, it\'s
essential to integrate data from digital enterprises with traditional
financial institutions\' data. This holistic approach ensures a
comprehensive assessment of the creditworthiness of rural populations.
Only through such integration can rural credit data become reliable and
help address the issue of information asymmetry among rural households.
Therefore, achieving interoperability between digital credit reporting
data and the central bank\'s credit system and developing a unified
credit system are essential steps.

**7.3.4 The Development of Rural Digital Finance, Both Online and
Offline, is not yet Synchronized**

Various digital financial companies have ventured into the rural market,
leveraging their online advantages to compensate for the shortcomings of
traditional rural financial institutions. In some ways, this has
facilitated the realization of rural inclusive finance. However, their
\"strong online, weak offline\" characteristic has, to some extent,
hindered their expansion in rural areas. Currently, cash transactions
remain a significant demand in rural areas, and handling cash
transactions still requires physical offline locations. Since rural
branches are relatively limited, rural residents may find it difficult
to withdraw funds from digital accounts, making the scarcity of physical
banking locations a bottleneck for the comprehensive expansion of
digital financial services.

For e-commerce platforms engaged in digital financial services in rural
areas, some of their offline financial service physical locations are
inadequate, making it challenging to provide offline-to-online (O2O)
financial services. This underscores the fact that, while digital
financial services are rapidly innovating online, the development of
offline service infrastructure lags behind. For example, the e-commerce
platform Meituan lacks rural agent locations, and it cannot effectively
integrate its offline business with its online services. This results in
situations where online services, such as discount coupons, may not be
used in rural areas. The lack of deep integration between online and
offline aspects of rural digital financial services is a significant
factor that affects and hinders rural customers\' access to
comprehensive financial services and the achievement of inclusive rural
finance.

**7.3.5 Lack of Government Legislation and Regulatory Oversight**

**There are two main issues related to the lack of government
legislation and regulatory oversight in the field of rural digital
finance:**

1.Incomplete Legal Framework: The existing laws and regulations in China
primarily focus on traditional rural financial institutions. These
regulations provide clear standards for regulatory entry and ongoing
supervision of traditional financial services. However, with the
introduction of \"digital+\" in the rural finance sector, which brings
new elements like digital finance, additional regulatory frameworks are
needed to address emerging risks. China has yet to establish a
comprehensive regulatory system for digital finance and rural areas,
which creates a gap in effective oversight. Although the \"Guidance on
Promoting the Healthy Development of Digital Finance\" was issued in
2015 to address policy gaps in digital finance, its specific
institutional design remains unclear. Furthermore, specific rules for
access and regulation of various digital financial services have not
been established, and supervisory mechanisms are still under
development. In the absence of comprehensive regulatory measures, it
becomes challenging to protect the rights of parties involved in legal
disputes. To ensure a more rational and standardized rural inclusive
finance system, it is essential to establish an appropriate regulatory
environment and enhance oversight over rural financial institutions,
ultimately using legal means to ensure its proper and standardized
development.

2.Unclear Regulatory Authority: The newly established Inclusive Finance
Department within the China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission
(CBIRC) in early 2015 has become the regulatory authority for the P2P
internet lending sector. Its regulatory scope also extends to areas like
microfinance, rural finance, and serving vulnerable groups, which will
have a significant impact on the development of rural inclusive finance.
However, with the complexities introduced by \"digital+\" in the rural
finance market, which involves various participants and a diverse range
of financial services, the determination of regulatory authority becomes
more complex. The interplay of finance and digital technologies blurs
the lines, leading to uncertainty about the appropriate regulatory
entity and, in some cases, the absence or misplacement of regulatory
actions. Therefore, it is crucial to promptly identify the regulatory
authority for rural inclusive finance in the context of \"digital+\" and
ensure effective regulatory oversight.

**7.3.6 Insufficient Awareness of Digital Technology and Digital Finance
Among Farmers**

Currently, there is a lack of digital knowledge and awareness among
rural residents. According to the 2018 \"China Internet Development
Status Statistical Report,\" rural populations make up the majority of
non-internet users, and their lack of digital knowledge and awareness
remains a primary reason for rural residents not using the internet. As
of December 2017, there were 611 million non-internet users in China,
with 37.6% in urban areas and 62.4% in rural areas. Lack of internet
skills and educational limitations are significant barriers to
non-internet usage. The survey showed that 53.5% of non-internet users
do not go online because they lack knowledge of computers or the
internet, and 38.2% have limitations in knowledge, such as not
understanding Pinyin. Additionally, 9.6% of non-internet users don\'t
use the internet because they don\'t need it or are not interested,
while 14.8% cannot go online due to the unavailability of computers or a
lack of digital infrastructure in their local areas. Therefore, rural
residents have insufficient awareness of digital technologies, are
unable to comprehend and operate complex software programs, and this
limitation hinders the development of digital rural financial services.

Furthermore, financial education in rural areas is relatively
underdeveloped, and there is inadequate promotion of financial
knowledge. As a result, financial literacy among rural residents is low.
Most rural residents are only familiar with traditional financial
services and have limited knowledge and trust in new digital financial
products. They often have reservations about digital financial products
and prefer to conduct their business with familiar traditional financial
institutions. This lack of understanding and trust in digital financial
products hinders the adoption of digital financial services in rural
areas and poses a challenge to the development of rural inclusive
finance.

**Chapter 8 Recommendations for the Application of Digital Technology in
Rural Financial Development**

To promote the better service of rural digital inclusive finance for
rural revitalization, one must approach innovation and management with
an objective and rational mindset. It\'s crucial not to blindly rush
forward merely by seeing opportunities or become paralyzed by problems
without taking any action. A tolerant attitude towards development is
required. This involves establishing mechanisms for \"tolerance\" and
\"trial and error\" to ensure the sustainability of innovation.

On one hand, the government should establish a comprehensive framework
for compensating innovation risks, providing fiscal subsidies, and
offering organizational support. On the other hand, financial
institutions should refine clauses that exempt them from liability in
the case of non-performing loans, encouraging their employees to engage
in bold innovations. They should also create comprehensive contingency
plans to make necessary adjustments promptly when risks arise.

It\'s essential to align with the current situation and policies for
development. A thorough consideration of objective situational and
policy risks is necessary. In-depth analysis of the Chinese and
international economic and political conditions is crucial, as well as
keeping up with relevant national policies. Currently, as the structural
reform of the agricultural supply side progresses, national attention to
digital finance increases, supportive policies emerge, rural digital
financial infrastructure improves, and services penetrate further, the
development of the \"digital + finance\" model will become a driving
force for rural economic development. Therefore, as market participants,
digital financial institutions must seize the opportunities in these
trends, tap into the rural market, and identify effective forms and
focus points for providing digital financial services in rural areas.
This will ensure the healthy advancement of agricultural supply-side
structural reform and the cultivation of new economic drivers.

Throughout this process, the key players in rural finance, the rural
financial system, and market regulatory agencies should all introduce
relevant reforms and innovations. They need to establish a well-rounded,
multi-level, diversified, and moderately competitive rural financial
system, ensuring the healthy and rapid development of rural finance.
This section provides recommendations in five areas to foster the
development of rural digital finance.

**8.1 Strengthen the Construction of Digital Infrastructure in Rural
Areas**

To strengthen rural internet and mobile communication network
infrastructure, providing the basic conditions for the effective use of
the internet in rural areas and improving the internet penetration rate
among rural households is essential. Digital infrastructure serves as
the entry point for various stakeholders in the rural financial market.
However, rural areas currently face issues with underdeveloped digital
infrastructure, and there is still a gap in internet penetration
compared to urban areas. Therefore, accelerating the dissemination of
digital applications in rural areas is crucial to truly drive the
development of rural inclusive finance.

Improving rural digital infrastructure involves two main aspects:

1\. Strengthening Policy Guidance: Government departments should provide
policy support for rural digital infrastructure development. Special
subsidy funds can be established to subsidize users who access the
internet. Rural areas often have challenging geographical conditions and
dispersed populations, resulting in higher costs for digital
infrastructure construction, maintenance, and usage compared to urban
areas. Given that rural residents typically have lower income levels,
government subsidies for rural internet users can help reduce the cost
of accessing the internet. Additionally, the government can offer
favorable policies to network operators to lower the construction costs
of rural digital infrastructure.

2\. Combining Construction with Application: It\'s important to ensure
that rural residents effectively apply digital technology. This can be
achieved through education, guidance, and improving the digital
financial literacy of rural residents. Encouraging rural households to
make rational and effective use of digital finance is essential.

By strengthening rural digital infrastructure and taking these steps,
the goal is to promote the development of rural inclusive finance and
ensure that rural areas are well-equipped for the digital age.

**8.2 Providing Digital Financial Products and Services that are
Tailored to Rural Needs**

**8.2.1 Accelerating the Integration of Rural Traditional Financial
Institutions and Digital Finance**

Traditional financial institutions face limitations in penetrating rural
markets. To address this, they need to undergo digital transformation,
establish a strong presence in rural areas, enhance the digital literacy
of their staff, and integrate digital finance concepts and technologies
into traditional financial services. By leveraging their existing
strengths, they can innovate and develop services such as mobile banking
and online banking. They can also explore fully automated processes for
online loan application, approval, and disbursement, providing
convenient and efficient financing services to rural households and
small businesses. Furthermore, financial regulatory authorities should
provide policy support to traditional financial institutions, while
ensuring risk control. This support may include relaxing entry
requirements for financial products like wealth management and loans in
rural areas. Additionally, approvals for village banks to offer services
like bank cards, bank acceptance bills, online banking, and mobile
banking should be expedited. These measures will enhance the competitive
capabilities of traditional financial institutions, allowing them to
expand their digital finance operations effectively.

**8.2.2 Layered Deployment of Traditional Financial Institutions and
Emerging Financial Institutions**

Traditional financial institutions have substantial advantages in terms
of network presence, capital, and risk management in rural markets.
Therefore, on the one hand, it is necessary to deepen the reform and
innovation of rural financial institutions. Enhancing and improving
rural finance involves encouraging agricultural development banks,
agricultural banks, postal savings banks, and similar institutions to
strengthen their support for rural development. It includes increasing
credit allocation to agricultural and rural areas, advancing rural
credit reforms, improving capital supplementation mechanisms, and
enhancing corporate governance to consolidate and elevate their
positions as major players in rural financial services. In addition,
there is a need to nurture and develop new rural cooperative financial
organizations, promoting the growth of community-based rural financial
mutual aid groups.

On the other hand, digital finance is rapidly expanding in rural areas,
offering a variety of innovative, data-driven financial services that
can bridge the last mile in financial services. Digital finance provides
efficient, convenient, and low-cost solutions to issues related to
small-scale loans. Therefore, during the period of economic
transformation, the country should accelerate the integration of digital
technology with rural finance to promote the role of rural digital
finance in addressing rural development issues. First, it is essential
to transition rural residents from offline to online usage by leveraging
mobile phones and traditional word-of-mouth methods. This shift not only
accomplishes mobile finance but also accelerates the development of
rural e-commerce, online investment attraction in rural areas, and the
promotion of financial products, all while addressing the issue of rural
credit that traditional financial institutions find challenging. Second,
there is a need to strengthen rural communication infrastructure.
Digital finance relies on modern communication technology, and the
current network coverage in rural areas, particularly in the central and
western regions, remains weak.

However, whether traditional or digital finance, neither can dominate
the rural financial market. The real goal of inclusive finance can only
be achieved by combining the depth of traditional finance with the
breadth of digital finance. This requires strengthening cooperation
between digital finance and traditional financial institutions, with
complementary advantages, collaborative innovation, and non-competitive
approaches. Presently, third-party payment is the preferred method
during the consumption process. Rural financial institutions should
collaborate with third-party payment internet companies to expand their
business and enhance efficiency. Similarly, digital financial companies
should leverage the policy and scale advantages of traditional financial
institutions in the rural market. Therefore, rural digital finance
should take a cooperative approach with traditional financial
institutions to share merchant resources and customer information. They
should establish comprehensive financial service stations, combining
banking, insurance, securities, and wealth management, in rural areas to
provide round-the-clock financial services. In the rural financial
market, many traditional financial institutions have set up convenient
financial service points in rural small shops and supermarkets. Although
the number of these service points is still limited and quite basic,
they can serve as offline agents for digital financial institutions.
Digital financial institutions can develop O2O financial service
platforms based on rural customer demands and the reality of rural
economies, using traditional financial institutions as agents through
their channels.

**8.3 Strengthen the Construction of Rural Digital Financial Service
Platforms**

**8.3.1 Enhancing the Rural Big Data Credit System**

Credit is the guarantee for the effective operation of all financial
services, especially in the field of rural digital finance, where the
establishment of a credit information system is of paramount importance.
Currently, in rural digital finance, various platforms utilize their own
credit standards, resulting in significant disparities in credit data.
This lack of standardization and data sharing hinders the industry\'s
development and leads to low costs of default for digital financial
services, increasing the occurrence of issues like malicious fraud and
fund embezzlement.

It is recommended that the country establish unified and improved credit
standards based on the unique characteristics of various digital
financial enterprises. This standardization aims to facilitate
industry-wide data sharing within digital finance. With the continuous
enhancement and harmonization of credit standards among digital
financial enterprises, active efforts should be made to ensure
comprehensive integration of digital finance into the credit information
system of the People\'s Bank of China (PBOC). This involves establishing
mechanisms for cooperation and credit information sharing between
digital finance and traditional finance, enabling more extensive sharing
of credit information.

The PBOC\'s credit information system does not currently include credit
information from rural areas and households. Rural digital finance
platforms collect, organize, and process credit information on rural
households using their services. Therefore, integrating the digital
finance credit information system with the PBOC\'s credit system can
mutually reinforce and complement each other, effectively preventing
unique risks associated with digital finance and systemic risks in the
overall financial market.

**8.3.2 Establishing a Rural Financial Risk Prevention System**

Rural digital finance risks are becoming increasingly diverse and
complex. Risk prevention is crucial for the development of rural digital
inclusive finance. Compared to urban financial markets, rural financial
markets are less developed in terms of standardization and the
regulatory environment. Market participants in rural areas generally
have weaker risk awareness and control capabilities. Digitalization can
amplify financial risks in rural areas, leading to more extensive risk
propagation. Hence, a higher level of risk prevention is required for
rural digital finance.

Some internet companies can leverage their advantages in data and
platform access to establish specialized risk prevention and control
departments. They can make full use of big data mining technology for
comprehensive risk prediction, defining appropriate risk assessment
criteria to detect risk vulnerabilities in a timely manner and reduce
the likelihood of actual risks occurring.

Moreover, the digital transformation of rural finance has introduced
many new financial products, along with new financial risks. Rural
digital financial enterprises must strike a balance between product
innovation and risk control to avoid over-innovation that might
exacerbate risks. Participants in rural digital finance should improve
their ability to recognize risks, conduct business operations in secure
online environments, download online banking and payment apps primarily
through legitimate channels or official websites, ensure the safety and
reliability of payment terminals, use a combination of payment passwords
and SMS verification, and raise awareness about protecting personal
privacy, account information, passwords, and related data. Additionally,
rural residents should educate themselves about various forms of digital
financial fraud, enhancing their ability to identify problematic
financial products and platforms.

**8.3.3 Improving Rural Digital Financial Supervision and Legal System**

\(1\) Enhancing the Rural Digital Financial Regulatory System

For rural inclusive finance, rural communities not only need equal
access to financial services but also require an equivalent regulatory
system. Currently, there are differences between internet finance
regulation and traditional finance regulation in rural financial
markets. In 2014, the People\'s Bank of China issued the \"China
Financial Stability Report (2014),\" which explicitly defined five
principles for internet finance regulation. It emphasized the need to
balance government regulation with self-regulation and make full use of
industry self-regulation. This serves as a guideline for establishing
the rural digital financial regulatory system.

The 2017 \"Number One Document\" for the first time called for the
severe crackdown on illegal fundraising and financial fraud in rural
areas and actively promoted rural financial legislation. Therefore, on
the one hand, internet finance institutions should actively adhere to
the central policy, strengthen internal control, proactively accept
regulation, and implement self-regulation. On the other hand, relevant
regulatory authorities should coordinate and cooperate with each other,
targeting illegal and irregular practices, including illegal fundraising
and financial fraud in rural internet finance. Regulatory policies
should be tailored to the rural context and encompass different
industries, scales, types, and other factors. This will create a
comprehensive regulatory framework and legal regulations for internet
finance.

To address the issue of unclear regulatory authorities in rural digital
finance, it is recommended to continue using the existing regulatory
system, \"one bank and three commissions\" (People\'s Bank of China,
China Banking Regulatory Commission, China Securities Regulatory
Commission, and China Insurance Regulatory Commission), as the main
regulatory authorities. These bodies can jointly formulate regulatory
systems for the digital finance industry. The existing regulatory system
can adopt a classification-based regulatory approach for digital
finance, differentiating regulation according to various forms and
scales, and also innovating regulation in combination with traditional
financial market regulatory methods.

Alongside clarifying regulatory authorities, industry self-regulation
should be strengthened, providing an effective complement to digital
financial regulation. It is suggested to establish a Digital Financial
Regulatory Association. The association can formulate relevant
self-regulations, rules, and systems. The participating entities in the
association should voluntarily comply with the industry\'s norms and
standards, strictly refrain from misconduct, such as illegal
fundraising, illegal deposits, or embezzlement of customer funds,
actively share relevant industry information, and achieve mutual
benefits, shared development.

\(2\) Improving the Legal and Regulatory Framework

In China, the legal and regulatory framework for the digital finance
sector is currently lacking. Additionally, the existing legal and
regulatory framework, based on traditional finance, is not well-matched
with modern digital financial regulatory concepts. This has led to an
unclear legal status for digital finance, frequent illegal activities,
and challenges for the stable development of digital finance. Therefore,
China should expedite the legislative process for digital finance to
standardize it and provide strong legal support for digital financial
institutions.

It is recommended that relevant regulatory authorities clarify market
positioning, business scope, supervision, and management for different
digital financial models. They should issue targeted and multi-layered
laws and regulations for various digital financial models, enabling
different digital financial entities to understand their development
directions clearly.

Furthermore, when formulating institutional rules, consideration should
be given to the differences between urban and rural areas. This will
require local governments to leverage their regional advantages and
establish well-defined entry rules and exit mechanisms for rural digital
finance, creating a secure environment for the development of rural
digital inclusive finance.

**8.4 Enhance Government Support**

**8.4.1 Strengthening Government Support for the Development of Rural
Digital Financial Enterprises**

Accelerate the government\'s introduction of guidance and incentive
policies related to rural digital finance, enhance the practicality of
these policies, and establish corresponding evaluation mechanisms.
Government policy formulation should adhere to the principles of
\"fairness, openness, and justice,\" and be in line with the actual
conditions of rural digital finance. It is essential to gather input
from various stakeholders, make the policy-making process transparent
and open. Moreover, during the policy implementation phase, the
government should strengthen communication efforts to increase policy
awareness among rural digital finance enterprises. This ensures that
these enterprises can timely understand, become familiar with, and
master the policies. The \"Guiding Opinions on Promoting the Healthy
Development of Digital Finance\" explicitly states that \"For
small-scale businesses, particularly those in the early stages, that
meet the conditions for China\'s existing tax policies for small and
micro-enterprises, they may enjoy specified tax preferential policies.
Combining the financial sector\'s shift from business tax to value-added
tax, digital finance tax policies should be comprehensively improved.
The implementation of tax deductions for expenses related to new
technology and product development by business entities should be
carried out. In addition to enjoying the above-mentioned tax incentives,
rural digital finance enterprises should consider preferential policies
related to agricultural taxes and fees. Besides existing policies,
indirect incentives such as delayed tax payments and investment offsets
can be adopted. Maintaining the stability of tax policies will provide
more advantages to rural digital finance enterprises. Furthermore, the
government can establish a dedicated fund to support the development of
rural digital finance enterprises and reward well-performing
enterprises, promoting the quality and efficiency of rural digital
finance development.

**8.4.2 Strengthening the Training of Rural Digital Financial Talents**

The digital finance industry does not require talent resources and
institutions to be located in the same place. It can overcome the
shortage of talent in rural areas compared to urban areas. The
government can build upon the existing financial professionals or
digital finance professionals to cultivate multi-disciplinary talents
for the rural financial market.

On one hand, the emphasis should be on providing relevant training for
professionals in the field of digital finance. The government should
regularly organize training programs for rural financial professionals
in digital technology to develop a workforce suitable for the rural
financial market.

On the other hand, the focus should be on nurturing the innovative
capabilities of these professionals. Currently, various digital
financial products are emerging in rural areas, and customer demands are
becoming more diverse. Rural digital finance enterprises should, in
accordance with national policies and regulations, continually innovate
financial products that align with market needs and are suitable for
rural communities.

**8.5 Enhance Financial Literacy and Education in Rural Areas**

1\. The first step is to use a wide range of media channels, such as
television and magazines, to disseminate basic financial knowledge to
rural areas. Tailor financial education and awareness campaigns to
different rural population groups. In addition, all participants in
rural digital inclusive finance should enhance training for farmers in
various financial activities. This may involve assigning staff to
provide one-on-one assistance to help them navigate the digital
platforms and practice the processes. Encourage farmers to conduct
various rural digital financial transactions online, allowing them to
experience digital finance firsthand and increasing their engagement.

2\. The second step is to cultivate financial risk awareness among rural
communities. Focus on financial risk prevention and awareness campaigns,
especially in the context of rural digital financial service innovation,
and raise awareness about the risks involved. Place special emphasis on
disclosing information related to financial consumer rights and risk
warnings. This will guide financial consumers in making rational
investment and consumption decisions based on their own risk tolerance
and the risk characteristics of financial products.

# Chapter 9 Research Conclusion and Contribution

To promote rural revitalization and achieve common prosperity, it is
essential to accelerate the reduction of disparities between urban and
rural areas in terms of income, consumption, basic public services, and
more, ensuring that rural residents truly experience increased
prosperity. In 2022, the Central Comprehensive Deepening of Reform
Committee deliberated and approved the \"Implementation Opinions on
Promoting High-Quality Development of Inclusive Finance.\" These
opinions emphasize the need to address the financial service gaps for
new types of agricultural entities and promote the orderly development
of digital inclusive finance. The aim is to empower rural inclusive
finance with digital technology, enabling financial products and
services to better reach rural areas, serve farmers, and facilitate the
sharing of development outcomes among rural populations.

High-quality rural digital inclusive finance can lower transaction costs
for farmers accessing financial services, enhance the inclusiveness of
rural finance, and address the challenges of difficulty and high costs
in obtaining financing for both farmers and agricultural development.
This, in turn, provides effective financial support for promoting
healthy and sustainable agricultural and rural development, contributing
to rural revitalization. To foster the sustainable development of rural
digital finance, it is essential to establish a modern rural financial
system that aligns with the needs of the rural context. Therefore,
research on rural finance and digital finance holds both theoretical and
practical significance.

This study is based on a fixed-effects panel data model and draws on
existing literature regarding digital and rural finance. It adopts a
combined qualitative and quantitative research approach and utilizes
panel data and information from the years 2009 to 2016. The research
approach combines theory, empirical analysis, and case studies to
explore issues related to digital and rural finance.

In the theoretical analysis, the study first discusses the current state
of rural finance development in China and arrives at the following
conclusions:

1\. The development of rural finance in China is crucial due to the
increasing demand for funds driven by the scale of agricultural
development. As urbanization accelerates and the rural population
declines, coupled with the \"separation of rights\" policy, the trend
toward the concentration of land operations has become established.
Large-scale development has created a demand for funds, making the
development of rural finance vital for promoting the large-scale and
efficient development of agriculture.

2\. Favorable policies continuously support the rapid development of
rural finance. Rural land reform has created a new market for
agricultural financial services, with multiple support policies
facilitating the expansion and innovation of agricultural finance.

3.  Currently, China\'s rural finance development faces several
    challenges, including weak basic conditions for rural financial
    services, information channel deficiencies in rural finance markets,
    a prevalence of single financial products in rural areas, dilemmas
    faced by both borrowers and lenders in rural finance, deviations in
    the lending direction of rural financial institutions, and a lack of
    synergy among various levels of financial institutions that could
    support rural development.

4.  Through in-depth research, this paper analyzes the fundamental
    reasons that hinder rural finance development at the levels of
    infrastructure, market, and government. It identifies the root
    causes of rural finance exclusion: at the infrastructure level, the
    scattered nature of rural natural villages makes infrastructure
    development challenging; at the market level, high financial service
    costs lead to \"market failure\"; at the government level, low
    regulatory efficiency results in \"government failure.\"

However, as digital technology continues to mature, does its application
substantively contribute to the development of rural finance? In the
empirical research, this study is based on panel data fixed-effects
models and analyzes data on the extent of rural internet penetration and
credit data of rural financial institutions for eight years from 2009 to
2016, covering all 29 provinces and municipalities in China, along with
other economic, financial, and social data. The empirical results
confirm the research hypothesis: the level of rural financial
development is positively correlated with the coverage of rural internet
users within various provinces in the country, indicating that the
proliferation of digital applications in rural areas and among rural
households is a significant factor associated with the positive
development of rural finance. Further research reveals that in regions
with higher per capita income levels, the positive association of
digital application proliferation among rural households on the
development of rural finance is stronger.

In the qualitative analysis of real-world applications, this study first
elaborates on three dimensions: the advantages of digital finance,
application patterns, and current issues. Firstly, this research
summarizes how digital finance leverages its advantages to promote rural
finance development, primarily from four perspectives: (1) digital
platforms help improve rural financial infrastructure; (2) digital
finance enhances the accessibility of financial services for rural
households; (3) digital finance lowers the cost of rural financial
services; and (4) digital technology improves the regulatory capacity of
rural finance, aligning with the previous discussion of the reasons for
the lag in rural financial development, explaining the mechanism by
which digital application promotes rural finance development.

Secondly, in terms of application patterns, as digital finance is
applied in rural finance development, several key patterns have emerged,
including the downward integration of comprehensive e-commerce channels,
the creation of an agricultural and financial integrated ecosystem by
agricultural leading enterprises, the expansion of rural financing
channels through agricultural internet finance, and the development of
\"digital + rural finance\" by traditional financial institutions. The
first three represent the financialization of internet companies, while
the fourth represents the digitization of financial institutions.

Finally, this study summarizes the current issues of digital finance in
rural development, mainly focused on four areas: weak rural digital
infrastructure, diversity of rural digital financial risks, the need for
improvement in rural big data credit systems, asynchronous development
of rural digital finance online and offline, lack of government
legislation and regulation, and inadequate understanding of digital
applications and digital finance among rural residents.

In summary, the proliferation of digital technology in rural areas and
among rural households significantly is a significant factor associated
with the inclusive development of rural finance. Digital technology
plays a positive role in promoting rural finance from three main
aspects:

1\. Reducing Transaction Costs: The widespread adoption of digital
technology helps financial institutions reduce transaction costs in
rural financial services. For instance, it effectively lowers customer
acquisition costs in credit services, enabling rural residents to access
financial service information without leaving their homes. Additionally,
the proliferation of digital applications helps financial institutions
save time and costs associated with approval processes and ongoing
management, thus facilitating the development of rural finance.

2\. Mitigating Information Asymmetry: Digital technology applications
can diminish information asymmetry in rural finance. These technologies
enable financial institutions to access more comprehensive and
widespread information about rural households, which traditional credit
methods may find challenging to obtain. This overall increase in
information transparency reduces information asymmetry in rural
financial transactions.

3\. Altering Borrowing Behavior: The widespread adoption of digital
applications in rural areas may significantly change rural households\'
borrowing behavior and their willingness to access financial services.
Rural residents may shift from traditional informal lending practices to
seeking more formal and convenient financial services, thereby altering
the subjective demand for rural financial services.

Based on the conclusions drawn from this study, the paper offers five
policy recommendations to harness digital technology for the healthy and
rapid development of rural finance. These recommendations include
strengthening rural digital infrastructure, providing digital financial
products tailored to rural needs, establishing a safety net for rural
inclusive finance, enhancing government support, and promoting financial
knowledge dissemination in rural areas.
