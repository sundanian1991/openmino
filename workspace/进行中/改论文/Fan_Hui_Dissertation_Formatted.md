# ABSTRACT

Since the reform and opening-up policy, especially since the 18th National Congress of the Communist Party of China, China's rural financial reform has continuously deepened. It has actively played a role in supporting agriculture, rural areas, and farmers, achieving positive results in supporting the precise implementation of poverty alleviation strategies, realizing the comprehensive development of rural areas, and advancing comprehensive rural revitalization. The 20th National Congress of the Communist Party of China proposed the goal of "comprehensively promoting rural revitalization" and the need to "improve the rural financial service system," providing further guidance for the reform and development of rural finance in the new era. Currently, in light of the diverse and multifaceted financial service demands required for rural modernization, China's rural financial sector still needs to address some deep-rooted issues, such as inadequate and outdated infrastructure, incomplete and difficult-to-collect agricultural data, insufficient supply of rural financial products and services, and a pressing need to enhance service efficiency and quality.

Digital rural development is a strategic direction for rural revitalization, and the exploration of the integration of digital technology and rural finance holds significant importance in practical financial services for rural revitalization. Long-term observation shows that the application of digital technology in rural finance can effectively address long-standing issues in rural financial development, such as high transaction costs, information asymmetry, and difficulties in financing. This has great significance in promoting rural revitalization, fostering shared prosperity, and reducing the urban-rural wealth gap.

In this dissertation, centering on the application of digital technology in rural financial development, we firstly analyze the challenges and reasons for the difficulties in rural financial development in China. Through empirical research, the paper concludes that there is a positive correlation between the level of rural financial development and the coverage of rural internet users in various provinces across the country. This suggests that the widespread use of digital technology in rural areas and among rural households is associated with a higher level of the positive development of rural finance. The paper also discusses the advantages of rural financial digitalization from a theoretical perspective and explains the mechanisms through which it promotes development at the infrastructure, market, and policy levels.

Secondly, we expound on the applications of Internet in rural finance. There are four major applications of Internet: comprehensive e-commerce channel sinking, building an integrated agricultural financial ecosystem composed of leading agricultural enterprises, interlinking of large P2P platforms with the agricultural supply chain, and traditional financial institutions developing the "Digital Plus Rural Finance" business mode.

Finally, while the application of digital technology can effectively drive the new development of rural finance, it still faces challenges and constraints. These include the lack of a robust rural credit system and issues related to the accessibility and utilization of new digital financial products and services in remote rural areas. To address these challenges, it is necessary for the government to accelerate research and implement a coordinated plan for further rural digital infrastructure, strengthen the innovation of digital inclusive financial products, and enhance the digital financial literacy of rural residents. This will allow the full potential of digital technology tools in rural finance to be realized. This dissertation, based on the use of rural financial digitalization to support the future development of rural revitalization, provides practical and feasible development recommendations with significant guiding value.

**Key words:** Digitalization; rural finance; rural credit; Rural Revitalization

# CHAPTER 1

## INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Research Motivation

The implementation of the rural revitalization strategy and the innovation of rural financial services in the new era are highly compatible. Rural revitalization cannot do without the support of rural financial innovation, and, conversely, it can promote the development of rural finance. The mutual promotion and organic unity of these elements will strongly drive sustainable rural economic development. On one hand, rural financial innovation offers an important guarantee for achieving rural revitalization by providing new financial products, payment methods, and financing channels. On the other hand, rural revitalization expands the overall size of the rural real economy, thereby providing a solid economic foundation for rural financial innovation.

In China, although rural financial institutions are not yet fully developed, the government has made significant efforts to address rural financial issues. However, instances of "market failure" and "government failure" persist, especially in traditional rural financial models. Issues such as capital outflows and "expensive financing" hinder the comprehensive implementation of the rural revitalization strategy. To address these shortages, the concept of "inclusive finance" was introduced, yet a significant gap remains between rural financial development and inclusivity. The 20th National Congress of the Communist Party of China further emphasized the need to improve the rural financial service system. Under this backdrop, the smooth progress of rural financial digitalization is key to providing financial support for rural revitalization. This study delves into the relationship between rural finance and digital applications from a theoretical perspective, aiming to create social value by integrating theory with practice. The study aims to understand how digital finance can effectively support rural finance, providing developmental insights and theoretical support for various fields.

### 1.2 Methodology and Results

This dissertation, based on an extensive reading of relevant literature and books, explores the relationship and methods of using digital technology to develop rural finance. The research approach integrates theory and practice to ensure that the suggested improvements are effective.

The study adopts a literature review method, where the paper extensively collects and organizes a wide array of books, literature, and data related to the application of digital technology in rural financial development. This synthesis establishes a strong theoretical foundation and supports the formulation of reasonable solutions. Furthermore, the paper employs quantitative analysis to examine the impact of digital technology adoption on rural financial development. To validate the hypothesis that higher levels of digital technology adoption are positively associated with better rural financial development, the study uses empirical data from Chinese rural areas from 2009 to 2016 for quantitative empirical analysis. Additionally, the paper utilizes a case study method to investigate the development of rural financial digitalization in the context of rural revitalization. Through case analysis, the paper derives relevant insights and formulates recommendations for using digital technology to promote rural inclusive finance.

The research concludes that there is a positive correlation between the level of rural financial development and the coverage of rural internet users in various provinces across the country. The empirical results effectively validate the basic assumption of this study, suggesting that the widespread use of digital technology in rural areas and among rural households is a significant factor associated with the positive development of rural finance.

Through empirical research, the paper demonstrates the positive relationship between digitalization and rural finance, and provides an explanation of the operational mechanism through which digitalization promotes development at its roots. The research confirms that digital applications significantly improve and optimize rural financial innovation, helping to activate the endogenous capabilities of farmers for income growth and enhance agricultural production efficiency. The analysis also reveals the causes of the current state of rural financial development from the angles of infrastructure, market, and government.

### 1.3 Possible Innovations and Limitations

The significance of this research primarily encompasses both theoretical and practical values. The innovation in this paper is summarized in several aspects: firstly, an innovative research perspective that delves into the endogenous relationship between financial demand and supply; and secondly, an innovative theoretical framework that focuses on the advantages of digital applications in rural finance. This foundation provides a fresh approach to constructing theoretical models, making the empirical findings more convincing. This paper tackles a cutting-edge research topic, as there is currently a lack of mature research findings regarding the concept of digital rural finance.

However, the study is subject to certain limitations. Due to constraints in the completeness of rural financial data and the characteristics of digital application data, which are frequently updated and extensive, this paper faced significant challenges in data selection. The effectiveness and accessibility of both sets of data were constrained, posing substantial interference with the empirical research. It is hoped that further studies can be conducted in the future to expand upon these existing conclusions.

## REVIEW OF LITERATURE

### 2.1 Western Studies

#### 2.1.1 Studies on Digital Finance

In Western countries, digital finance does not exist as a separate sector. Most Western scholars and industry experts assert that digital finance is a part of the financial sector and thus largely embodies the functions of financial intermediary. Digital finance emerged from practice and is a newborn thing, so little theoretical research has been done on digital finance. Digital finance appeared earlier in Western countries, where many scholars and experts have ever introduced their own views on digital finance. Leland and Pyle (1977) proposed the concept of financial intermediary, claiming that financial intermediary is an alliance that can facilitate information sharing. Later, many experts and scholars worked on improving the concepts of financial intermediary and digital finance. For instance, Diamond (1983) argued that financial intermediary alliance is composed of individual investors. Merton and Bodie (1993) recognized the potential of digital finance, demonstrating that financial intermediaries represented by digital finance could accurately collect and process information, which is the precondition for a good financial institution to function properly. Diamond and Rajan (2001) reported that financial intermediaries pay more attention to their reputation and possible financing margins when fulfilling their duties. In the following year, Berger and Udell conducted a monographic study on the contractual relationship between financial intermediaries and enterprises, reporting that counterparties under whatsoever relationships are, without exception, convinced of the importance of information. With continuous research and attempt as well as the upgrade of financial products, modern financial theory is gradually taking shape.

In recent years, as digital finance evolved by leaps and bounds, digital finance in developed countries such as Europe and the United States has gradually become standardized, institutionalized and self-regulated. Especially after President Obama signed the *Jump Start Our Business Startups Act* (hereinafter referred to as the JOBS Act) in 2012, digital finance is getting mature in Europe and the United State. There has been growing interest in digital finance and some valuable research findings have been reported by more experts and scholars. These studies mainly deal with the development and mode of digital finance, the characteristics and transaction behaviors of borrowers, lender behaviors, and risk and regulation.

Studies on the development of digital finance. Western studies on the development of digital finance include: Meyer and Heng (2007) introduced the concept of P2P. Reil (2007) contended that the application of Web 2.0 technology provides a technical support for the development of digital financial platforms. Slavin (2007) stated that the application of digital technology has significantly curtailed and even eliminated the operating cost of online lending platforms, the transaction cost of customers, the time cost of counterparties and the intermediation cost of mortgage guarantee.

Johnson (2010) found that the low-cost network technology has fostered a potential large customer base for the popularization of P2P lending. Berger (2007) theorized that digital financing platforms can help to reduce information asymmetry and achieve win-win for both borrowers and lenders. Wang (2009) stated that the financing mode of integrating e-commerce with finance will reshuffle the existing loan market. Studies on the transaction behaviors of digital finance. There are abundant studies on the transaction behaviors of digital finance abroad, most of which focus on the behaviors of the participants in P2P lending.

Studies on borrower behaviors. Ryan (2008) asserted that borrowers' financial and non-financial information may influence the loan approval rate. According to Freedman and Jin (2008), a borrower may find it easier to get a loan and close the transaction if he furnishes the investor more precise and detailed financial information. In the view of Herzenstein (2008), the borrower's financial strength is an important factor underlying the loan approval rate. Klafft (2008) found that the loan/income ratio and debt ratio exert a significant impact on the loan interest, whereas the proof of collateral ownership has little impact on loan pricing. Klafft (2008) also found that credit score has a significant impact on the loan approval rate. Iyer et al. (2009) supported that conclusion. Krumme and Herrero-Lopes (2009) argued that the higher the credit score, the easier it will be for the borrower to get a loan. Puro (2010) contended that the loan approval rate is negatively correlated with the loan amount and interest rate. Collier (2010) supported that argument. Kumar (2007) found that investors recover risk compensation from borrowers depending the non-performing loan ratio. Freedman & Jin (2008) also asserted that default notes would lower the borrower's credit rating and in turn influence the loan application, loan rate and loan amount. Ravina (2008) maintained that demographic characteristics (race, sex, age, etc.) may influence the transaction behaviors in the P2P lending market. Pope (2008) further confirmed this conclusion. Pope & Sydnor (2008) classified discrimination into taste-based discrimination and statistical discrimination. Pope & Sydnor (2008), Ravina (2012), Pope & Sydnor (2008), Pope & Sydnor (2008), Chen (2014) and Barasinska (2009) studied borrower behaviors from the aspects of race, age and gender respectively, concluding that these demographic characteristics may significantly influence the loan approval rate and loan rate. On the contrary, Andres (2008), Pope & Sydnor (2008), Ravina (2012) and other scholars argued that demographic characteristics have a negligible impact on the loan approval rate compared with the borrower's financial factors. In addition, some scholars have studied non-financial information except demographic characteristics. Lin (2009) examined the relationship between loan purpose and loan approval rate and loan rate. Ana Ceci-lia (2008) and Michels (2012) suggested that P2P lending is accompanied with information filtering cost. Herzenstein et al. (2011) found that borrowers' self-recommendation is negatively correlated with investors' willingness to lend. Kumar (2010) argued that group reputation has no significant impact on "good" borrowers.

Researchers on lender behaviors. Kumar (2010) claimed that investors' behaviors conform to the "rational-economic man" hypothesis. Sonenshein (2010) found that social capital has a significant impact on investors' decision-making. Samuel (2008) reported that "search & bidding" is a key factor in investors' investment decision-making. Puro (2010) contended that herd mentality is commonly found with investors in the bidding process. Asch (1956) introduced the herd behavior effect of investors. Scholars like Krumme & Herrero (2009), Herzenstein et al. (2010), Ceyhan et al. (2011) and Lee et al. (2012) supported that herd behavior is common in P2P lending transactions. As to the reasons behind herd behavior, Krumme & Herrero (2009) found that once the first investor bids, the bidding process will speed up rapidly. Herzenstein et al. (2010) asserted that the amount of proposals is an important factor inducing herd behavior. In the view of Shen et al. (2010), social capital is also one of the factors causing herd behavior. Ceyhan et al. (2011) and Lee et al. (2012) found that the impact of participation rate on subsequent investment is diminishing. Zhang et al. (2012) argued that rational herd behavior exists in the United States' P2P lending market. And yet, Wang & Greiner (2010) and Luo & Lin (2013) asserted that irrational herd behavior exists in the market. Studies on social capital. Putnam (1993) introduced the concept of social capital. Some scholars investigated the impact of social capital of the lender in a P2P lending platform, namely "soft information", on the loan availability and cost. For example, Ming Fing Lin (2009) demonstrated that relational social capital can alleviate information asymmetry in the lending process. Greiner et al. further confirmed this conclusion. Freedman (2010) probed into the causes of information asymmetry in the P2P lending market. Sergio Herrero-Lopez (2009) examined the impact of social interaction on credit behaviors, concluding that social interaction can raise the loan approval rate. Studies on the model of digital finance.

Western studies on the operating model of P2P lending platforms mainly concentrate on those successful P2P lending platforms. Greiner & Wang (2009) and Lin et al. reported that the Prosper credit rating system is relatively mature with well-established pre-loan and post-loan rules. Hulme (2006) concluded through a case study of Zope that Zope provides transparent and real financial services. Emma (2011) made a comparative analysis on the different types of P2P platforms in Europe and concluded that Zope's success lies in risk control. Studies on the risks and regulation of digital finance. Freedman & Jin (2008) found that the borrower would deliberately conceal unfavorable information to induce the lender to grant loans, thereby intentionally producing information asymmetry and resulting in adverse selection. But with a wealth of trading experience, investors can easily discover such information asymmetry. Johnson (2010) argued that a group can also solve the problem of information asymmetry by establishing an appropriate incentive mechanism. Weiss et al. (2010) suggested that the "anonymity" of the digital exacerbates information asymmetry. Therefore, P2P lending platforms' verification of borrower information can substantially ease the problem of adverse selection. As to the studies on the regulation of P2P lending platforms, some scholars hold that the problem of P2P platform regulation must be solved.

Xiao Guang & Yi (2011) claimed that P2P platforms must be subject to financial regulation.

A brief comment on Western studies on digital finance: Western studies mainly deal with the development and mode of digital finance, the characteristics and transaction behaviors of borrowers, lender behaviors, and risk and regulation. The latest theoretical findings with respect to the development and mode of digital finance include: the concepts of P2P lending and crowdfunding were defined, and the application of digital technology and the mediating effect of Internet platform were investigated through case study of Prosper and Zope, striking a cornerstone for the rapid development of digital finance. The latest theoretical findings regarding the transaction behaviors in the digitalfinance market include: empirical studies on the transaction behaviors in the digital finance market from the borrower's perspective indicate that the more detailed the information furnished by the borrower, the stronger the financial strength, the higher the credit score, the clearer the use of loans and the better the group's reputation, the higher the loan approval rate and loan rate will be. However, the loan amount, capital price, default rate and borrower self-recommendation are negatively correlated with the loan approval rate and loan rate. In addition, demographic characteristics and gender are also related to the loan approval rate and loan rate. Empirical studies from the investor's point of view suggest the investor's behaviors conform to the "rational-economic man" hypothesis, herd behavior exists in the P2P lending market, and the impact of participation rate on subsequent investment follows the law of diminishing in economics. Equally important, the studies also imply that social capital, i.e., soft information, has a significant impact on the loan approval rate. The higher the softness of information, the more unreal and unreliable the signals sent, the lower the loan approval rate and the higher the loan rate will be. In other words, information softness is negatively correlated with loan approval rate and capital price. The latest theoretical findings regarding risks and regulation of digital finance include: Western studies on digital finance show that the "anonymity" of participants in the digital financial market exacerbate the risk of information asymmetry, but the problem of information asymmetry can be solved by establishing an incentive mechanism. In addition, researchers conclude through studies on the risks of digital finance that P2P lending platforms should be subject to financial regulation.

Generally speaking, Western studies on digital finance have yielded a considerable amount of research findings, providing a theoretical guidance for the sustainable and sound development of digital finance. However, there are also many limitations in these Western studies. Firstly, the studies on the development and mode of digital finance are primarily concerned with the development of P2P lending and crowdfunding, while few of them are attempted to explore other online financing models and study their development. Secondly, most scholars select Prosper and Zope as sample platforms, leading to poor representativeness and replicability of samples. Thirdly, Western studies on the participants in the digital financial market focus on particular participants but rarely probe into the participants' behaviors as a whole. Fourthly, there is still dearth of research on the prevention of risks associated with digital finance and regulation of online lending platforms.

#### 2.1.2 Studies on Rural Finance

There are few literatures on rural finance and rural economy in Western countries. And the representative ones mainly concentrate on the efficiency of financial instruments and financial risk aversion in developing countries. For example, Bain (1992) stated that micro financial efficiency mainly refers to the utilization of financial instruments, while macro financial efficiency refers to the contribution of the entire banking industry to macroeconomic stability. Galor, Zeira, Banerjee & Newman (1993) found that financial development and economic growth cannot bridge the income gap in underdeveloped financial markets, and this goal can be attained only by improving the rural financial market. In terms of financial instrument services, Frank Hllinger (2011) argued that although credit quotas and collaterals can be used to reduce the risks of rural financial instruments and operating costs, commercial financial institutions are apt to consider the problem of insufficient collateral and are less likely to provide more services to the rural financial market. Likewise, Meyer (2011) claimed that it is very difficult to provide financial services to rural areas in developing countries since financial institutions are less inclined to do so in consideration of interest cost. In fact, the biggest problem of providing services to the rural financial market lies in financial regulation and risk control. Steel and Andah (2008) surveyed the supervision over the microfinance market and concluded that the cost of supervision is too high. Gutirrez-Niet et al. (2007) analyzed the attributes of microfinance institutions, reporting that they not only have social attributes but also yield profits. They further concluded through quantitative analysis that their identity as non-governmental organization is the key factor affecting the utilization of microcredit funds. Again, some scholars stressed the necessity to regulate microfinance services with laws and regulations. Arun (2005) claimed that appropriate rules and regulations help to increase the efficiency of microfinance institutions and fulfill their diversified functions.

On the other hand, some scholars theorize that rural finance may influence agricultural output, and the efficiency of rural finance should be measured by the agricultural output it contributes. In 1990, Michael T. Belongia and R. Alton Cilbert created an analytical model for assessing the impact of rural finance on agricultural output in a bid to analyze the relationship between the two. In the same year, J. D. von Piske et al. developed a variety of functions and linear programming to test the relationship between rural finance and agricultural output. The in-depth analysis of scholars and experts has generated wide interest in rural finance from all sectors of society. In 2002, two heavyweights from the USDA Agricultural Research Service and the FED pointed out that rural finance needs to adapt to the financial market environment of the new age in the shortest possible time in order to facilitate economic growth and maintain social stability by serving the rural areas. In the same year, Indian scholars studied the impacts of rural banking policy changes in India over the past 40 years and found that the rise and development of rural banks contributed to significant improvements of the local economy. Despite of the wide recognition of rural finance, people also realize that quite a few problems with rural finance have yet to be addressed. In Jun. 2001, the Caribbean Development Bank explicitly pointed out that there are problems in operation of rural financial organizations which cannot be radically solved by government intervention. Many Western scholars believe that the studies on the rural financial system in developing countries are of high value considering the particularity of developing countries' economic development. And these studies have also made considerable achievements. Eze et al. (2010) analyzed the Nigerian government's agricultural financial policy, demonstrating that although the government has made a lot of policy efforts, the budget allocation to rural areas is still limited, which might lead to corruption. Junior R. Davis (1998) studied Romania's rural financial problems quantitatively, reporting that rural residents' income is the main factor affecting their loans and savings, in addition to some other factors such as the use of loans. John James (2013) investigated rural finance in China and pointed out that the corresponding fiscal and taxation policies should be analyzed at village, township and county levels. Jensen (2005) found through empirical studies that the government-led agricultural credit system in developing countries is relatively inefficient in promoting agricultural investment. On the contrary, the market-oriented financing mode combined with moderate and necessary intervention practiced in developed countries are more efficient.

### 2.2 Chinese Studies

#### 2.2.1 Studies on Digital Finance

Chinese studies on digital finance deal mainly with the development, models, transaction behaviors, risks and supervision of digital finance as well as the comparison with traditional banking and Western models.

Studies on the development of digital finance. Scholars have different opinions on the definition of digital finance. Xie Ping et al. (2012) introduced the concept of digital finance in *A Study on Digital Finance Model*, expounding on its connotation and extension and suggesting that digital finance is a "third" financing model different from commercial banking and capital market. Ma Yun (2013) argued that the engagement of digital enterprises in financing gives rise to digital finance, while the operation of traditional financial institutions creates financial networking. Wu Xiaoling (2013) contended that the essence of digital finance is to use the digital and information technology to process and transmit financial information, conduct financial operations, build channels and complete the accommodation of funds. Qian Jinye et al. (2012) claimed that P2P lending originated from the folk Rotating Savings and Credit Associations, only that it exists as a "platform" with the aid of digital information technology, that is, P2P lending platform. Ye Bing (2013) pointed out that with the rapid development of e-commerce, the financial services provided by digital enterprises to customers have expanded from single payment service to all the core services of finance. Regarding the reasons for the emergence of digital finance, Yang Tao et al. (2015) maintained that the development of digital finance is driven by complaints from the real economy sector about traditional finance, accumulation of private capital and blockage of investment channels. The integration of private financing with the digitalization gives birth to a new type of financial operation, stimulating the real economy sector to involve in financial operations and blurring the boundary of traditional financial sector. Li Jizun (2015) asserted that digital finance in China emerged amidst the evolution of information and communication technology, the booming of e-commerce, the inefficiency of traditional financial services and the sluggish of supervision. Liu Lanbiao et al. (2013) argued that digital finance is more likely to integrate with instead of replacing traditional banks. Dong Yun et al. (2014) firstly made a review of the studies on the development of digital finance and then analyzed the development trend and theoretical significance of digital finance, concluding that we should not only encourage innovation but also intensify regulation of digital finance. Qiao Haishu et al. (2014) reported that in addition to the existing literatures on digital finance in China, we should conduct more studies on the attributes, risk measurement and thinking of digital finance as well as dissimilation of power. Wang Yunquan et al. (2013) studied the impact of digital finance on the traditional services of commercial banks, finding that traditional banks should learn innovation from digital financial institutions and seek confluent development with them. Xie Qinghe (2013) proposed that we should promote complementary between e-commerce and traditional banking to accelerate the sustainable and sound development of digital finance. Huo Bing et al. (2015) argued that the development of digital finance is mainly driven by the application of big data, transaction scale, transaction exposure and transaction willingness. Wu Xiaoqiu (2015) suggested that the competition between digital finance and traditional finance will spur the "genetic" transformation of financial structure, improve the efficiency of financial resource allocation and boost the development of financial inclusion.
