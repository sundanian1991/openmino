Heritage · 170 Years

Six moments that shaped a uniform.

From a dry-goods store to a global second skin — the turning points of a 170-year craft.

1853
The Founding

Bavarian immigrant Levi Strauss opens a dry-goods store in San Francisco, supplying canvas and denim to Gold Rush miners.

1873
The Rivet Patent

With tailor Jacob Davis, Strauss patents the copper rivet — birth of the blue jean, the first workwear built not to tear.

1936
The Red Tab

A small red tab stitched into the back pocket becomes the first brand identifier ever sewn into a garment.

1980
The 501 Codified

The Lot 501 reaches its definitive form — button-fly, straight leg, the silhouette against which every other jean is still measured.

2011
Water<Less>

The Waterless program cuts up to 96% of the water from finishing — denim craft learning to respect its own resources.

2024
SecondHand

A dedicated pre-owned platform makes a 40-year-old 501 worth more than a new one — heritage as circular economy.

Values · Three Convictions

What 170 years insists on.

01

Craft

Every rivet, every felled seam, every indigo dip is a decision not to take the shortcut. Sturdiness is the brand's first language.

02

Democracy

The same jean worn by a miner, a president, and a teenager. Levi's made a garment that refuses to sort people by class.

03

Stewardship

From Waterless finishing to SecondHand resale, the company is learning to treat denim as a resource worth handing forward.

A garment is finished when the person wearing it decides it is. Until then, it keeps getting better.
